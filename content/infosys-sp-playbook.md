# Infosys SP / Power Programmer Complete Playbook — From HackerEarth Login to 11.25 LPA Joining Letter

Bhai, sun. **Infosys SP (Specialist Programmer)** aur **Power Programmer (PP)** — ye dono Infosys ke regular SE (Systems Engineer, 3.5-4.5 LPA) waali bhid se alag tier hain. Same campus, same shirt, same ID card — but salary literally **2x to 3x**. SP clears at **8-9 LPA**, PP at **11.25 LPA**. Iska scale dekh: India mein har saal **500,000+ aspirants** Infosys ka koi na koi test attempt karte hain — TCS NQT (1M+) ke baad ye second-largest single placement gateway hai. Aur ye sirf "Infosys" naam ka offer nahi hai — ye PP joiner literally same college mein same project pe baith ke same client ke liye kaam karta hai jaha L1/L2 SDE 22 LPA pe baitha hai. **CTC delta is real, role delta is mostly cosmetic in year 1.**

Ye playbook us aptitude playbook se alag hai jo tune already padha (`aptitude-quant`, `aptitude-logical`, `aptitude-verbal`). Wahan tu fundamentals seekha — formulas, shortcuts, vocabulary, RC strategy. **Yahan hum Infosys-specific strategic layer build karenge** — kaunse pseudocode patterns repeatedly aate hain, HackerEarth ka editor kaisa behave karta hai, SP test ke 5 sections mein time kaise allocate karna hai, PP-specific extra coding round mein graph / DP problem kaise approach karni hai, HR mein "DBMS / OS questions" technical-flavoured kaise milte hain, aur 90-din ka prep plan jo realistically execute ho sake. Yeh playbook + strategy doc hai. Fundamentals ke liye cross-reference bana hua hai, repeat content nahi karega.

## 1. Infosys SP / PP kya hai — aur regular Infosys se kaise alag hai

### 1.1 Three tiers, one company — first understand the ladder

Infosys campus hiring mein **teen distinct tiers** hain. Same company, same training centre (Mysuru), but offer letter pe printed CTC fundamentally alag hai.

| Tier | Designation | CTC | Test path | Role nature |
|------|-------------|-----|-----------|-------------|
| **Standard SE** | Systems Engineer | 3.5 - 4.5 LPA | Infosys via TCS NQT score / InfyTQ / Off-campus drive | Generalist; ILP training, then service / support project |
| **Specialist Programmer (SP)** | Specialist Programmer | 8.0 - 9.0 LPA | **Infosys SP test (HackerEarth)** | Coding-heavy roles; product / engineering teams |
| **Power Programmer (PP)** | Power Programmer | 11.25 LPA | **SP test + extra advanced coding round** | Top 10% of SP; advanced engineering / R&D / digital tracks |

**Critical clarification**: SP test alag hota hai. Ye **Infosys ka apna entrance** hai, conducted via **HackerEarth platform** (na ki Infosys-Mettl, na ki TCS iON). Ye ek dedicated test hai jiske registration links Infosys careers portal pe alag se aate hain — usually August-October window mein for upcoming graduating batch.

> **Galat dhaarna #1**: "TCS NQT pass kar liya to Infosys SP automatic mil jaata hai." **Galat.** TCS NQT score sirf standard Infosys 3.5 LPA SE role ke liye consider hota hai (off-campus pipeline). SP / PP ke liye **separate test dena padta hai.**

> **Galat dhaarna #2**: "PP koi alag test hai." **Galat.** PP candidate bhi same SP test deta hai. PP-specific differentiation ek **extra advanced coding problem** par hoti hai (90 min, 1 hard problem) jo SP test ke baad ya ke part mein conducted hoti hai. Top performers ko PP offer milta hai — same merit pool se.

### 1.2 SP vs PP — granular diff

| Dimension | Specialist Programmer | Power Programmer |
|-----------|----------------------|------------------|
| **CTC** | 8.0 - 9.0 LPA | 11.25 LPA |
| **Variable component** | Fixed mostly | Fixed + small performance |
| **Test rounds** | MCQs + Pseudocode + 2 coding | Same + 1 extra hard coding (90 min) |
| **Selection ratio** (approx) | ~5% of attempters | ~1-2% of attempters |
| **Role on joining** | App developer / coder / engineering team | Same kind of team but high-priority projects, R&D, digital |
| **Bond** | 2 years | 2 years |
| **Project track** | Java / .NET / Python full-stack typical | Polyglot, product engineering, digital transformation |

**Year-1 actual difference**: SP joiner aur PP joiner ko Mysuru ILP mein 14-22 weeks ka same training milta hai. PP wale ke liye sometimes accelerated track. Project allocation ke time PP wale ko **engineering-heavy** projects mein priority milta hai (Finacle, EdgeVerve products, internal platform teams), while SP wala typical client-facing dev role mein. **Technical work overlap 70%+ hai initial 1-2 years**.

### 1.3 Eligibility — pehle confirm kar le

Ye criteria SP / PP dono ke liye apply hote hain. Standard SE ke criteria slightly relaxed hote hain, but humara focus SP/PP pe hai.

| Criteria | Requirement |
|----------|-------------|
| **10th percentage** | 60%+ (75%+ recommended for PP shortlist) |
| **12th percentage** | 60%+ (75%+ recommended for PP shortlist) |
| **Graduation CGPA** | 6.5 / 10 minimum, **7.0+ strongly recommended for SP**, 7.5+ for PP |

Off-campus SP drive bhi hota hai (typically July-October), wahan strict college brand filter nahi hota — pure score matters.

### 1.4 When to attempt — the campus calendar

Infosys SP / PP typically late 3rd year ya early 4th year mein attempt karna hota hai (B.Tech ke context mein):

- **3rd year end (May-July)**: InfyTQ certification + DSE test (Digital Specialist Engineer 6 LPA) windows. Tu agar yahan top performer banta hai, SP-equivalent path kabhi kabhi unlock hota hai. But primary path nahi hai.
- **4th year start (August-October)**: **Main SP / PP campus / off-campus drive window**. Result + HR within ~4-6 weeks.
- **4th year mid (November-January)**: Re-attempts allowed for off-campus candidates who missed earlier window in some years.
- **4th year end (February-April)**: Joining letter follow-up; ILP date allocation.

> **Pro tip**: Pre-final year mein **HackerEarth pe ek free account bana le** abhi, aur platform ke editor + custom input + sample test cases ka feel le le. Bahut bachhe drive ke din pehli baar HackerEarth pe code likhte hain — woh 5-10 min wahi struggle mein chala jaata hai.

## 2. The full test structure — section-by-section dissection

### 2.1 SP main test — full breakdown

Infosys SP test runs on HackerEarth and has **5 sections**, totalling ~165 minutes (2 hr 45 min). Section-wise time and section-wise cutoff are both enforced — **you cannot redistribute time across sections.**

| # | Section | Q | Time | Time/Q | Difficulty |
|---|---------|---|------|--------|------------|
| 1 | Logical Reasoning | 15 | **25 min** | 100 sec | Medium |
| 2 | Quantitative Aptitude | 10 | **35 min** | 210 sec | Medium-Hard |
| 3 | Verbal Ability | 20 | **20 min** | 60 sec | Easy-Medium |
| 4 | **Pseudocode (the differentiator)** | 10 | **25 min** | 150 sec | Medium-Hard |
| 5 | **Hands-on Coding (HackerEarth)** | 2 | **60 min** | 30 min/Q | Medium-Hard |

**Total SP main test: ~57 questions + 2 coding in 165 min.**

> Note: section-wise question counts and times sometimes vary by ±2 questions or ±5 minutes between cycles. Pattern verified from PrepInsta + GeeksforGeeks 2024-2025 reports. Treat the table as the cycle-stable reference.

### 2.2 PP-specific extra round — the 90-min hard problem

Power Programmer candidates ko ek additional round dena hota hai. Format:

| Section | Q | Time | Type |
|---------|---|------|------|
| Advanced Coding (PP only) | 1 | **90 min** | Hard problem — graph / advanced DP / string algo |

This round either runs **immediately after SP main test** as a continuous slot (total ~4 hr 15 min including breaks) ya **separately within next 1-2 days** for shortlisted candidates. Cycle-by-cycle varies. Plan for the continuous-slot scenario by default.

### 2.3 Time-per-question reference table (memorise)

| Section | Questions | Time | Sec/Q | Cushion strategy |
|---------|-----------|------|-------|------------------|
| Logical | 15 | 25 min | 100 | Knock easy ones <60s, save time for DS / puzzle |
| Quant | 10 | 35 min | 210 | 10 questions in 35 min = 3.5 min/Q — **deceptive luxury**, questions are HARD |

Bachhe think karte hain "yaar luxury hai, time kafi hai" — and then Q3 ke time pe panic karte hain because the questions are noticeably harder than TCS NQT quant. ### 2.4 Cutoffs — section-wise + composite

Infosys doesn't publish official cutoffs (ye annual basis pe vary karte hain), but pattern from 2024-2025 cycles:

| Section | Approx cutoff | Implication of missing |
|---------|---------------|------------------------|

| Quant | ~50% | Auto-reject |
| Verbal | ~50% | Auto-reject |

### 2.5 Negative marking, navigation, and platform behaviour

- **Negative marking**: Generally **none** in Infosys SP MCQs (verified for 2024-2025 cycles). Galat answer = 0 marks, skip = 0 marks. So **always guess** — random guess gives 25% probability.
- **Section navigation**: Within a section you can navigate back-forward. **Once a section ends (timer expires or you click "next section"), you cannot go back.** Plan accordingly.
- **HackerEarth coding section navigation**: You can switch between the 2 problems freely. Both timers run on a shared 60-min budget.
- **Auto-submit**: Section-wise timer auto-submits when expired. Don't rely on manual submit.
- **Camera + screen-share**: SP test is proctored. AI proctoring + sometimes live human proctor. Window switching = flag. Phone visible = flag. Family member walking in = flag.

## 3. Pseudocode round — the secret weapon

10 questions in 25 minutes. **Yahi vo section hai jaha 70% candidates filter hote hain.** TCS NQT mein PLQ (Programming Logic Questions) similar hota hai (10Q / 15 min) — but Infosys pseudocode noticeably **harder** hai. Snippets longer, dry-runs deeper, recursion + bit manipulation + DP combos appear regularly. Tu agar TCS PLQ comfort se solve karta hai, Infosys pseudocode mein next-level thinking lagani padegi.

### 3.1 Question types you'll see

| Type | % of section | Example | Time per Q |
|------|--------------|---------|------------|
| **Output prediction (longer snippet)** | 35% | Trace through 15-line code, what does it print? | 2-3 min |
| **Loop / recursion dry-run** | 25% | What is `result` after this nested loop? | 2-3 min |
| **Time / space complexity** | 15% | Best-case, average, worst-case Big-O | 1-2 min |
| **Bug spotting / fix** | 10% | Which line is wrong? What's the corrected version? | 2 min |
| **Conceptual** (data structure / algo) | 10% | Which DS gives O(1) for X? | 1 min |
| **Operator precedence / type coercion** | 5% | What does `5 + 3 * 2 / 4` evaluate to? | 1 min |

Pseudocode language is **mostly C / Java syntax**, sometimes Python-like indentation. Tu agar Python-only coder hai, **C/Java syntax read karna padega** for output prediction — variable declarations, semicolons, `printf` / `System.out.println`, `for(int i = 0; i < n; i++)` style loops.

### 3.2 Topics that appear repeatedly (2024-2025 cycle analysis)

| Topic | Frequency | Sample question style |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Arrays + indexing edge cases** | High | What's printed for input `[3, 1, 4, 1, 5]`? |
| **Recursion** (factorial, Fibonacci, mutual) | High | Trace `f(4)` for given recursive function |
| **Strings** (slicing, reversal, substring) | High | What does `s.substring(2, 5)` return for s="hello"? |
| **Bit manipulation** | Medium | `x & (x-1)` does what? |
| **Basic DP** (memoization recognition) | Medium | What's the time complexity with/without memo? |
| **Pointers (C-style)** | Medium | `*p = b` modifies what? |
| **Operator precedence / shortcircuit** | Medium | `a++ + ++b` evaluates how? |
| **Switch fall-through** | Low | Without `break`, what happens? |
| **Static vs instance variables** | Low | After 3 calls, what does counter print? |

### 3.3 Top 12 traps (memorise — these eat your score)

1. **Operator precedence**: `int x = 5 + 3 * 2 / 4;` → 5 + (3 * 2 / 4) = 5 + 1 = 6 (integer division). Many candidates compute 5 + 3 * 0.5 = 6.5 — wrong because integer division.
2. **Post vs pre-increment**: `int a=5; int b = a++;` → b=5, a=6. `int b = ++a` → b=6, a=6.
3. **Integer overflow** (C / Java): `int` is 32-bit signed. `INT_MAX + 1` wraps to `INT_MIN`. Common in factorial / multiplicative recursion problems.
4. **Floating-point comparison**: `0.1 + 0.2 == 0.3` → **false**. (Result is 0.30000000000000004.)
5. **Java string `==` vs `.equals()`**: `==` compares references; `.equals()` compares content. `String a = "x"; String b = new String("x");` → `a == b` is **false**.
6. **Array out-of-bounds**: `int arr[5]` has valid indices 0-4. `arr[5]` is undefined behaviour in C, throws `ArrayIndexOutOfBoundsException` in Java.
7. **Recursion missing base case**: stack overflow.
8. **Recursion base case wrong direction**: `if (n > 0) return 1;` — base case fires too early or too late.
9. **Switch fall-through**: missing `break` → execution continues to next case.
10. **Logical short-circuit**: `if (p != null && p.next != null)` — if `p` is null, second clause not evaluated. Reorder = NullPointerException.
11. **Bitwise vs logical**: `5 & 3 = 1` (bitwise AND of 101 & 011 = 001), but `5 && 3 = true` (logical AND).
12. **`x & (x - 1) == 0`**: classic check for "is x a power of 2?". Easy to mis-trace. (Note: precedence — `==` binds tighter than `&` in C, so put parentheses.)

### 3.4 Ten worked pseudocode examples (the hands-on portion)

Each one is from real Infosys SP-style 2024-2025 patterns. Trace step by step.

#### Example 1: Factorial recursion with twist

```c
int f(int n) {
  if (n == 1) return 1;
  if (n == 0) return 1;
  return n * f(n - 2);
}
// Call: f(5)
```

**Trace**: f(5) = 5 * f(3) = 5 * (3 * f(1)) = 5 * (3 * 1) = **15**.

Trap: many candidates assume `f(n-2)` is `f(n-1)` (regular factorial), get 120. Read carefully.

#### Example 2: Nested loop output

```c
int n = 4, count = 0;
for (int i = 1; i <= n; i++) {
  for (int j = i; j <= n; j++) {
    count++;
  }
}
printf("%d", count);
```

**Trace**:
- i=1: j=1,2,3,4 → 4 iterations
- i=2: j=2,3,4 → 3 iterations
- i=3: j=3,4 → 2 iterations
- i=4: j=4 → 1 iteration
- Total: 4+3+2+1 = **10**.

Pattern: this is `n*(n+1)/2` for any n, equals 10 for n=4.

#### Example 3: Bit manipulation classic

```c
int count_bits(int x) {
  int count = 0;
  while (x) {
    x = x & (x - 1);
    count++;
  }
  return count;
}
// Call: count_bits(13)
```

**Trace**: 13 = 1101.
- x=1101, x-1=1100, x&=1100, count=1
- x=1100, x-1=1011, x&=1000, count=2
- x=1000, x-1=0111, x&=0000, count=3
- x=0, exit. Return **3**.

Trap: `count_bits` returns the **number of set bits**, not the value. Don't confuse with x itself.

#### Example 4: Recursion with two return paths

```c
int g(int n) {
  if (n <= 0) return 0;
  if (n == 1) return 1;
  return g(n - 1) + g(n - 2);
}
// Call: g(5)
```

**Trace**: This is Fibonacci. g(5) = g(4) + g(3) = 3 + 2 = **5**.

(Build up: g(0)=0, g(1)=1, g(2)=1, g(3)=2, g(4)=3, g(5)=5.)

#### Example 5: String reversal in pseudocode

```c
char s[] = "hello";
int n = 5;
for (int i = 0; i < n / 2; i++) {
  char tmp = s[i];
  s[i] = s[n - 1 - i];
  s[n - 1 - i] = tmp;
}
printf("%s", s);
```

**Trace**:
- i=0: swap s[0] and s[4]: "oellh"
- i=1: swap s[1] and s[3]: "olleh"
- i=2 (loop ends since 5/2 = 2)
- Output: **olleh**.

Trap: students write `for i < n` instead of `i < n/2` — would unswap and return original "hello".

#### Example 6: Operator precedence

```c
int a = 5, b = 10;
int c = a + b * 2 - 4 / 2;
printf("%d", c);
```

**Trace**: precedence = `*` and `/` before `+` and `-`. `c = 5 + (10*2) - (4/2) = 5 + 20 - 2 = **23**`.

#### Example 7: Pointer manipulation (C)

```c
int a = 5, b = 10;
int *p = &a;
int *q = &b;
*p = *q + 5;
b = a + 3;
printf("%d %d", a, b);
```

**Trace**:
- `*p = *q + 5` → a = 10 + 5 = 15.
- `b = a + 3` → b = 15 + 3 = 18.
- Output: **15 18**.

#### Example 8: Memoization recognition / complexity

```c
int memo[100];
int f(int n) {
  if (n <= 1) return n;
  if (memo[n] != -1) return memo[n];
  memo[n] = f(n-1) + f(n-2);
  return memo[n];
}
```

What is the time complexity of `f(n)`?

**Answer**: **O(n)**. Without memoization, Fibonacci recursion is O(2^n). With memoization, each subproblem solved once, so O(n) time and O(n) space.

#### Example 9: Static variable

```java
class C {
  static int counter = 0;
  void inc() { counter++; }
}
// In main:
C a = new C(); C b = new C();
a.inc(); a.inc(); b.inc();
System.out.println(C.counter);
```

**Trace**: `static` means counter is shared across all instances. a.inc() twice + b.inc() once = 3 increments. Output: **3**.

Trap: students assume each object has its own counter (would be 2 + 1 = different values). Static = class-level.

#### Example 10: Bug spotting

```c
int sum_array(int arr[], int n) {
  int sum = 0;
  for (int i = 0; i <= n; i++) {       // Line A
    sum += arr[i];
  }
  return sum;
}
```

**Bug**: Line A — `i <= n` should be `i < n`. With `i <= n`, the loop accesses `arr[n]` which is **out of bounds** (valid indices are 0 to n-1). Undefined behaviour, garbage value added to sum.

**Fix**: `for (int i = 0; i < n; i++)`.

### 3.5 Pseudocode time strategy

- 10 questions in 25 min = 150 sec average. Realistically:
  - **3 easy questions** (output prediction with simple trace) — 60-90 sec each. Bank time.
  - **5 medium questions** (recursion, bit manipulation, string trace) — 2-3 min each. Spend the budget here.
  - **2 hard questions** (DP recognition, complex pointer / static / bug) — 3-4 min each. Mark and skip if stuck >3 min.
- Always leave 1-2 min at the end for review.

**Target: 8/10 correct = 80%.** Below 60%, section cutoff missed → SP eligibility lost regardless of other scores.

## 4. Hands-On Coding Round (SP main test) — 2 problems / 60 min on HackerEarth

**This is the second decisive section.** 2 problems in 60 minutes on HackerEarth. Tu agar dono problems pass kara leta hai, you're golden — combined with decent MCQ scores, SP shortlist confirmed. Agar ek pass aur ek partial, still strong candidate. Dono fail = pure aptitude score useless, no SP offer.

### 4.1 Format and HackerEarth platform tips

- **Problems**: 2 problems, both medium difficulty. Sometimes "1 easy + 1 medium," sometimes "1 medium + 1 medium-hard." First problem ko hamesha pehle attempt karna recommended.
- **Languages allowed**: C, C++, Java, Python 3, sometimes JavaScript / Go (cycle dependent).
- **Editor**: HackerEarth's browser editor. Has basic syntax highlighting, no autocomplete (unlike VS Code), no tab-to-complete. Plan to type out everything manually.
- **Test cases**: Each problem has 5-15 test cases. **2-3 visible**, rest **hidden**. Visible cases are usually easy / sample inputs from the problem statement; hidden cases test edge conditions, large inputs, tricky bounds.
- **Custom input**: HackerEarth allows you to **paste your own input** and run code. Use this aggressively — type in edge cases yourself before submitting.
- **Partial credit**: Yes — passing 5/10 hidden cases gives 50% on that problem. Don't bail after seeing one fail; submit your best.
- **Compile errors**: Cost time but no negative marking. Compilation is slow on HackerEarth (~3-5 sec per compile). Aim for first-shot compile.

### 4.2 Why Python (for most candidates)

For SP main coding round, **Python 3 is the optimal language** for most candidates. Reasons:

- Fewer LOC than C / Java. 30-line Java solution = 12-line Python.
- Built-ins: `sorted()`, `set()`, `Counter`, `enumerate`, `reversed`, `defaultdict` — all save 10+ lines.
- String slicing: `s[::-1]` reverses in 1 line. Java needs `new StringBuilder(s).reverse().toString()`.
- List / dict comprehensions handle frequency-count problems in 1 line.
- No type declarations, no semicolons, no manual array sizing.

**Trade-off**: Python is 5-10× slower than C++ at runtime. For SP main test, input sizes are small (N ≤ 10⁵ typically), so Python TLE is rare. **For PP advanced (section 5), C++ is sometimes safer** — we'll address there.

### 4.3 Reading input — HackerEarth uses stdin

```python
# Single integer
n = int(input())

# Two space-separated integers
a, b = map(int, input().split())

# Array of N integers on one line
n = int(input())
arr = list(map(int, input().split()))

# 2D matrix R rows, C cols
r, c = map(int, input().split())
grid = [list(map(int, input().split())) for _ in range(r)]

# String input
s = input().strip()  # always .strip() to drop trailing newline

# Multiple test cases (T at top — common pattern in HackerEarth)
t = int(input())
for _ in range(t):
    n = int(input())
    arr = list(map(int, input().split()))
    # solve
```

**Edge cases**:
- Always `.strip()` strings — HackerEarth sometimes leaves trailing whitespace.

### 4.5 Five worked solutions (Python) with complexity + edge cases

#### Problem 1: Second-largest unique element

> Given an array of N integers, find the second-largest **unique** element. If no second-largest exists (all same / only one unique), print -1.

```python
n = int(input())
arr = list(map(int, input().split()))

unique = list(set(arr))
if len(unique) < 2:
    print(-1)
else:
    unique.sort(reverse=True)
    print(unique[1])
```

**Time**: O(N log N) (due to sort). **Space**: O(N).
**Edge cases**: all same elements → -1; single element → -1; negative numbers handled by `sort(reverse=True)`.

#### Problem 2: Anagram check

> Given two strings, determine if they are anagrams (same characters, same frequencies, ignoring case).

```python
from collections import Counter
s1 = input().strip().lower()
s2 = input().strip().lower()
print("YES" if Counter(s1) == Counter(s2) else "NO")
```

**Time**: O(N). **Space**: O(K) where K is alphabet size.
**Edge cases**: different lengths → directly NO (Counter handles this); spaces matter unless explicitly stripped — clarify with the problem statement.

#### Problem 3: Maximum subarray sum (Kadane's algorithm)

> Given an array, find the contiguous subarray with the maximum sum. Print the sum.

```python
n = int(input())
arr = list(map(int, input().split()))

max_so_far = arr[0]
max_ending = arr[0]
for i in range(1, n):
    max_ending = max(arr[i], max_ending + arr[i])
    max_so_far = max(max_so_far, max_ending)
print(max_so_far)
```

**Time**: O(N). **Space**: O(1).
**Edge cases**: all negative array → answer is the largest single element (which Kadane's handles correctly).

Trap: students initialise `max_so_far = 0` — fails when all elements are negative (would return 0, expected: largest negative).

#### Problem 4: Coin change — minimum coins

> Given coin denominations `[c1, c2, ..., ck]` and amount `A`, find minimum coins needed to make amount A. Print -1 if not possible.

```python
n = int(input())
coins = list(map(int, input().split()))
amount = int(input())

INF = float('inf')
dp = [INF] * (amount + 1)
dp[0] = 0

for i in range(1, amount + 1):
    for c in coins:
        if c <= i and dp[i - c] != INF:
            dp[i] = min(dp[i], dp[i - c] + 1)

print(dp[amount] if dp[amount] != INF else -1)
```

**Time**: O(amount × number_of_coins). **Space**: O(amount).
**Edge cases**: amount = 0 → 0 coins; impossible amount (e.g., coins = [2, 4], amount = 3) → -1.

#### Problem 5: Two-sum (using hashmap)

> Given an array of integers and a target sum K, return indices of two numbers that add up to K. Assume exactly one such pair exists. (1-indexed output.)

```python
n, k = map(int, input().split())
arr = list(map(int, input().split()))

seen = {}  # value -> index
for i, x in enumerate(arr):
    if k - x in seen:
        print(seen[k - x] + 1, i + 1)  # 1-indexed
        break
    seen[x] = i
```

**Time**: O(N). **Space**: O(N).
**Edge cases**: same number twice as a pair (e.g., arr = [3, 3], k = 6) — handled because we check `seen` *before* adding current.

### 4.8 HackerEarth-specific platform pitfalls

- **Tab vs spaces in Python**: HackerEarth's editor sometimes inserts tabs when you press Tab. Mixed tabs+spaces = `IndentationError`. Use 4 spaces consistently; copy-paste from the same indent style.
- **Print statements during debugging**: Remove all debug `print` calls before submitting. HackerEarth strict-matches output; extra lines = WA on every test case.
- **Trailing newlines**: `print` in Python automatically adds `\n`. Don't add `\n` manually.
- **Input prompt strings**: Don't pass prompts to `input()` (e.g., `input("enter n: ")`). HackerEarth doesn't strip them — output mismatches.
- **Importing libraries**: Standard Python 3 libraries are available (`collections`, `math`, `heapq`, `bisect`, `re`, `itertools`, `functools`). NumPy / pandas usually NOT available.
- **Recursion depth**: Python's default recursion limit is 1000. For deep recursion (e.g., DFS on 10⁵-node graph), increase with `sys.setrecursionlimit(10**6)` at the top.

### 5.3 Three worked PP-level problems

#### PP Problem 1: Number of Islands (BFS on grid)

> 2D grid of '1' (land) and '0' (water). Count number of connected islands (4-directionally).

```python
import sys
from collections import deque
input = sys.stdin.readline

r, c = map(int, input().split())
grid = [list(input().strip()) for _ in range(r)]

def bfs(i, j):
    q = deque([(i, j)])
    grid[i][j] = '0'
    while q:
        x, y = q.popleft()
        for dx, dy in [(-1, 0), (1, 0), (0, -1), (0, 1)]:
            nx, ny = x + dx, y + dy
            if 0 <= nx < r and 0 <= ny < c and grid[nx][ny] == '1':
                grid[nx][ny] = '0'
                q.append((nx, ny))

count = 0
for i in range(r):
    for j in range(c):
        if grid[i][j] == '1':
            bfs(i, j)
            count += 1
print(count)
```

**Time**: O(R × C). **Space**: O(R × C) worst case (BFS queue).
**Why BFS over DFS**: For large grids (R × C > 10⁵), recursive DFS may hit Python's recursion limit. Iterative BFS with deque is safer.
**Edge cases**: Empty grid (R or C = 0); all-water grid → 0; all-land grid → 1.

#### PP Problem 2: 0/1 Knapsack (advanced DP)

> N items, each with weight `w[i]` and value `v[i]`. Knapsack capacity W. Each item can be taken 0 or 1 time. Find maximum value.

```python
n, w = map(int, input().split())
weights = list(map(int, input().split()))
values = list(map(int, input().split()))

# Optimised: 1D DP rolling array
dp = [0] * (w + 1)
for i in range(n):
    for j in range(w, weights[i] - 1, -1):  # iterate backward to avoid using same item twice
        dp[j] = max(dp[j], dp[j - weights[i]] + values[i])

print(dp[w])
```

**Time**: O(N × W). **Space**: O(W) — optimised from O(N × W) using 1D rolling array.
**Why iterate backward**: For 0/1 knapsack (each item once), updating `dp[j]` from `dp[j - weight[i]]` requires `dp[j - weight[i]]` to still represent the *previous* iteration's value. Iterating j from high to low ensures this. (Forward iteration → unbounded knapsack — same item used multiple times, different problem.)
**Edge cases**: W = 0 → 0 (can't take anything); all items weight > W → 0.

#### PP Problem 3: Longest palindromic substring (expand around centre)

> Given a string S, find the longest palindromic substring. If multiple, return any one.

```python
s = input().strip()
n = len(s)
if n == 0:
    print("")
else:
    start, max_len = 0, 1

    def expand(l, r):
        while l >= 0 and r < n and s[l] == s[r]:
            l -= 1
            r += 1
        return l + 1, r - 1  # inclusive bounds of palindrome

    for i in range(n):
        # Odd-length palindrome centred at i
        l1, r1 = expand(i, i)
        # Even-length palindrome centred between i and i+1
        l2, r2 = expand(i, i + 1)
        if r1 - l1 + 1 > max_len:
            start, max_len = l1, r1 - l1 + 1
        if r2 - l2 + 1 > max_len:
            start, max_len = l2, r2 - l2 + 1

    print(s[start:start + max_len])
```

**Time**: O(N²). **Space**: O(1).
**Why expand-around-centre over Manacher's**: Manacher's is O(N) but harder to code under 90-min time pressure. O(N²) for N up to 10⁴ runs comfortably in Python; for N up to 10⁵, switch to Manacher's or C++.
**Edge cases**: Empty string → ""; single character → that character; all-same string ("aaaa") → entire string.

#### Q3: CS Fundamentals — DBMS

Common patterns:
- "Difference between primary key, candidate key, foreign key?"
- "What is normalisation? Why 3NF?"
- "Index — when does it help, when does it hurt?"
- "Difference between DELETE, TRUNCATE, DROP?"

> **Model (index)**: "An index is a data structure (typically B-tree) that the database maintains alongside a table column to speed up `WHERE` queries on that column. It helps when you query a small fraction of rows from a large table — e.g., looking up by user-id in a 10M-row users table goes from O(N) full scan to O(log N) index lookup. It hurts on insert / update / delete because the database has to update the index too — so over-indexing a write-heavy table degrades performance. #### Q4: CS Fundamentals — OS

Common patterns:
- "Difference between process and thread?"
- "What is deadlock? How to prevent?"
- "Paging vs segmentation?"
- "Context switching — what happens?"

> **Model (process vs thread)**: "A process is an independent execution unit with its own memory space — code, data, heap, stack. A thread is a lightweight execution unit *within* a process; threads of the same process share code, data, heap, but each has its own stack and registers. Inter-process communication (IPC) requires explicit mechanisms like pipes, shared memory, sockets — slower. Inter-thread communication is direct (shared memory) but requires synchronisation primitives like mutexes to avoid race conditions. Process creation is expensive (fork system call), thread creation is cheap. Trade-off: processes are isolated (one crash doesn't kill others), threads are not."

#### Q5: CS Fundamentals — OOPS

Common patterns:
- "4 pillars of OOP?"
- "Difference between abstract class and interface?"
- "Method overriding vs overloading?"
- "Why is multiple inheritance not allowed in Java?"

> **Model (abstract vs interface)**: "Abstract classes can have both implemented methods and abstract methods (declared without body), and they can have instance variables and constructors. A class can extend only one abstract class. Interfaces, in pre-Java-8, could only declare methods — all implicitly public abstract — and have only `public static final` constants. From Java 8 onwards, interfaces can have `default` methods with implementations, and a class can implement multiple interfaces. Use abstract class when classes share state or partial implementation; use interface when classes share only a contract / behaviour without inheritance hierarchy."

#### Q6: PP-specific — System Design

For PP candidates, expect a 5-10 min system-design-flavoured discussion. Don't need to be a senior engineer; just show structured thinking.

> **Sample prompt**: "How would you design a URL shortener like bit.ly?"

> **Model framework**:
> 1. **Clarify requirements**: read-heavy? Write-heavy? Custom aliases allowed? Expiration? Analytics?
> 2. **Estimate scale**: 100M URLs / month → 1.2B / year → ~12B over 10 years. With 7-char base62 encoding (62^7 = 3.5T), we have headroom.
> 3. **Core API**: `POST /shorten {long_url}` → `{short_url}`. `GET /{short_id}` → 301 redirect.
> 4. **Database**: SQL (Postgres) for durability, key = short_id, value = long_url + metadata (created_at, click_count). For 1.2B/year, single DB → shard by short_id hash.
> 5. **Caching**: Redis in front for hot URLs (top 1% of URLs serve 90% of traffic).
> 6. **Short-id generation**: counter-based with base62 encoding (1 → "a", 62 → "1A", etc.) OR random with collision check.
> 7. **Analytics**: async log to Kafka → ClickHouse / S3 for batch reporting.
> 8. **Edge cases**: malicious URLs (blacklist check), expiration (TTL on DB row), rate limiting per IP.

For full system design depth → `system-design-basics`, `system-design-advanced`.

#### Q7: "Are you okay with the 2-year service bond?"

The bond is real. ₹1.5-2 lakh penalty if you leave before 2 years. **In interview, accept warmly.** Don't argue.

> **Model**: "Yes, completely. Infosys's training program — Mysuru ILP — is itself worth the commitment. I see 2 years as a learning runway. By the time the bond completes, I'll have 1.5+ years of project experience plus certifications, which sets up the next decade of my career. I'm not in this for a short-term offer."

Privately:
- 2 years gives you ILP (3-6 months) + ~1.5 years of project experience. Strong resume signal.
- Many SP / PP joiners switch to product companies (Microsoft, Atlassian, Adobe, Goldman) at the 2-year mark with 1.5-2× hike.
- If you must leave early (medical / family), bond can sometimes be waived on documented hardship.

#### Q8: "Any questions for me?"

**ALWAYS have 2-3 prepared.** Saying "no" looks disinterested.

> **Models**:
> - "Could you share what kind of projects new SPs typically get assigned to in their first 6 months post-ILP? Are these client-facing or internal product engineering?"
> - "How does the SP / PP role progression look in 2-3 years — are there opportunities to switch into specialised tracks like AI, Cloud Architecture, or product management?"
> - "What's the most exciting tech transformation Infosys is investing in right now that an SP might contribute to?"

### 7.4 Red-flag answers (auto-disqualify in Infosys HR)

- "I just need a job, any job." Honest but disqualifying for SP role.
- "I'll join Infosys but I'm planning to do an MS in 1 year." Don't volunteer.
- "I prefer Bangalore only." Be flexible — Infosys posts SP joiners to Bangalore, Mysuru, Pune, Hyderabad, Chennai.
- "I don't know much about my project, my teammate built that part." Project ownership matters.
- "I haven't really used DBMS / SQL." Even if true, frame as "limited usage in projects, but I've covered the fundamentals through coursework."
- "Salary is low compared to product companies." True but irrelevant in HR.

### 7.5 Communication checklist

- Smile (yes, even on Teams).
- Eye contact (look at camera, not the screen of yourself).
- Pace your speech — don't rush, especially when explaining technical concepts.
- 2-second pause after each question; don't rapid-fire.
- "Thank you" at start and end.
- Wear a collared shirt; clean background; good lighting.
- Have your resume open in another window for quick reference if asked about a specific project / certification.

### 10.1 Pre-test 24-hour checklist

- [ ] Confirm test slot, HackerEarth login link works, password remembered.
- [ ] Restart laptop. Update Chrome (Infosys SP usually requires Chrome / Edge).
- [ ] Webcam + mic tested via Zoom / Teams test page.
- [ ] Backup internet (mobile hotspot ready, tested).
- [ ] ID proof in pocket (Aadhar primary).
- [ ] Formula sheet glanced (don't cram).
- [ ] Pseudocode trap list (section 3.3) reviewed.
- [ ] Vocabulary list reviewed.
- [ ] Plain wall room set up; lighting from front (not behind).
- [ ] Water bottle filled.
- [ ] Family informed: "do not disturb between X-Y; do not enter room."
- [ ] Sleep 8 hours night before. **No all-nighter.**
- [ ] Eat a light meal 90 min before test.
- [ ] Login 30 min early.
- [ ] HackerEarth platform — open in tab, ensure no blocked extensions / pop-up blockers active.

### 10.2 During test

- Open with the section you're strongest at — momentum matters.
- Mark-and-skip aggressively for any question taking >2× target time.
- Don't look at the clock every 30 sec — every 5 min is fine.
- Hands-on coding: brute-force first, optimise later.
- For pseudocode: dry-run on paper, not in head.
- If you panic: 30-second deep breath, drink water, restart that question fresh.
- For PP advanced: spend first 10-15 min on understanding only — don't rush to code.

### 10.3 After test — what to learn next

Once you've cleared SP / PP and got the offer letter — the real grind begins. ILP starts in 2-3 months. Use that time productively. Recommended sequence:

- **`tcs-nqt-playbook`** — if you're attempting both Infosys SP and TCS NQT in parallel (~70% prep overlap). Cross-reference time-strategy.
- **`aptitude-quant`** — for any retake or deeper drilling on quant fundamentals.
- **`aptitude-logical`** — full reasoning depth, especially DS + statements + puzzles.
- **`aptitude-verbal`** — RC technique, 11 grammar modules, vocabulary expansion.
- **`dbms-complete`** — Infosys interviews probe SQL queries + normalisation + indexing. Critical for HR + ILP project allocation.
- **`os-complete`** — process / thread / deadlock / paging — common HR + ILP topics.
- **`networks-complete`** — OSI / TCP-UDP / HTTP — sometimes asked in PP-track HR.
- **`java-fundamentals`** — most Infosys projects are Java-heavy. Even if your stack is different, Java basics give you ILP runway.
- **`spring-boot-basics`** — for the most common Infosys backend stack post-ILP.
- **`resume-behavioural`** — STAR framework, behavioural answers, resume polish, salary negotiation, LinkedIn optimisation. Critical before HR.
- **`system-design-basics`** + **`system-design-advanced`** — for PP candidates and any future product-company switch.
- **`design-patterns`** + **`clean-code-solid`** — directly tested in PP HR and Infosys ILP graduation projects.
- **DSA practice on LeetCode** — 100 LeetCode Easy + 50 Medium + 10 Hard before joining gives you 18-month head start over peers.

### 10.4 The mindset

Bhai, ek last baat. Infosys SP / PP pass karna **achievement** hai, but it's not the destination. It's the *runway*.

Tu agar SP banta hai, congratulations — top 5-10% of Indian engineering campus pool mein aa gaya, irrespective of college brand. PP banta hai — top 1-2%. Infosys ILP (Mysuru) tujhe corporate environment + project rigour + structured stack training degi. Bond ke 2 saal mein tu real-world enterprise codebase pe kaam karega — service-mesh deployments, mainframe integrations, cloud migrations, banking platforms. Ye experience product company fresher (Atlassian / Amazon / Microsoft) ko nahi milta first 2 years mein.

Aur ek baar tu ne 1.5-2 saal complete kiye, **choices unlock ho jaate hain**:
- Internal lateral to Infosys digital / EdgeVerve product teams.
- Switch to product companies — usually 1.5-2× hike (8 LPA → 14-16 LPA SDE-1, 11.25 LPA PP → 22-25 LPA SDE-2).
- MS / MBA abroad with strong corporate experience on resume.
- Domain specialisation — banking tech (Finacle), retail tech, telecom, healthcare.

11.25 LPA PP offer dekh ke "kam hai" mat soch. Tu agar ye top 1-2% slot achieve karta hai, **2-year compounded outcome 30-40 LPA** ka hai. That's how careers actually compound — not in a single offer letter.

Lage raho. Test crack kar. SP banja, PP target rakh. Joining letter le. Mysuru chala ja. Phir agle subject pe focus shift kar — DBMS, OS, system design — taaki 2 saal baad tu *choose* kar sake aage kya karna hai, nahi ki *settle* karna pade.

## 1. What TCS NQT actually is — history aur 2026 reality

### 1.1 Naam ka itihaas — NQT vs NQTm vs NQT-IT

TCS ne 2018 mein **NQT (National Qualifier Test)** launch kiya as a single common entrance for hiring across all functions. Pehle har stream ka alag test tha — IT, Finance, BPS — chaos tha. NQT ne sab merge kar diya. Phir 2020 mein TCS ne segmentation start kiya:

- **TCS NQT** (the umbrella) — open to all students, all streams, all colleges. Free, online, proctored.
- **TCS NQT-IT** (renamed to **TCS Smart Hiring** internally) — specifically for IT services hiring. Most engineering students target this.
- **NQTm** — Management graduates ke liye, MBA / BBA / BCom. Different syllabus, irrelevant for engineers.

2024 se TCS ne pattern ko further refine kiya: ek single test hota hai, but score brackets decide karte hain ki tu **Ninja**, **Digital**, ya **Prime** offer ke liye eligible hai. Tu ek hi test deta hai — multiple offers ke liye qualify ho sakta hai. Ye TCS ka USP ban gaya — "one test, multiple opportunities."

### 1.2 2026 structure — Foundation + Advanced

Current pattern (verified from TCS official iON portal and PrepInsta 2024-2025 cycles):

**Foundation Section** (mandatory, decides Ninja eligibility):
- Numerical Ability — 25 questions in 25 min
- Verbal Ability — 25 questions in 25 min
- Reasoning Ability — 30 questions in 50 min
- Programming Logic (PLQ) — 10 questions in 15 min
- Hands-on Coding — 2 problems in 45 min

**Total Foundation: ~92 questions + 2 coding in 160 min (2h 40min).**

**Advanced Section** (optional but mandatory for Digital / Prime):
- Advanced Reasoning — 15 questions in 25 min
- Advanced Coding — 1 problem in 90 min

**Advanced adds another 115 min** — so full test (Foundation + Advanced) = ~4h 35min including breaks.

### 1.3 Score thresholds — kaunsa tier mil raha hai

TCS doesn't publish exact cutoffs (deliberately — woh year-by-year vary karte hain), but the publicly known pattern from 2023-2024 cycles:

| Tier | Foundation Cutoff | Advanced Required? | CTC | Role |
|------|-------------------|--------------------|----|------|
| **Ninja** | ~60-65% Foundation | No | 3.36 LPA | Systems Engineer |
| **Digital** | ~75% Foundation + Advanced cleared | Yes | 7.00 LPA | Digital Engineer |
| **Prime** | ~85% Foundation + Advanced + Interview top quartile | Yes | 9.00-11.50 LPA | Prime Engineer |
| **CodeVita** | Separate test (not NQT) | N/A | Direct interview pipeline | Top performers can land Digital/Prime |

> **Pro tip:** Agar tu Ninja target kar raha hai aur Advanced skip kar raha hai — galti hai. Advanced attempt karna **free** hai, agar tu Foundation mein 60% laaya tab bhi Advanced ke marks count nahi hote against you. Advanced pass kar diya = Digital ka offer khul gaya — same effort, 2x salary.

### 1.4 On-campus vs off-campus

- **On-campus drives**: Tier-1 / Tier-2 colleges mein TCS direct visit karti hai. NQT score still required, but additional internal cutoffs college-specific hote hain. **Round 1 = NQT, Round 2 = Technical Interview, Round 3 = HR.**
- **Off-campus drives**: TCS iON portal pe register kar. Same NQT, same scoring, same offers. Off-campus interview slots usually delayed by 4-6 weeks compared to on-campus. **More candidates, same selection ratio.**

Off-campus mein advantage: tu jab chahe attempt kar (TCS NQT runs 4-5 cycles per year). Disadvantage: koi college-level relaxation nahi milega — pure score matters.

### 1.5 Eligibility — pehle confirm kar le

| Criteria | Requirement |
|----------|-------------|
| **10th percentage** | 60%+ |
| **12th percentage** | 60%+ |
| **Graduation %** | 60% / CGPA 6.0+ |
| **Backlogs (active)** | 0 active at the time of joining |

**Service bond**: 1.5 years from date of joining. Break karega to ₹50,000 - ₹1 lakh penalty (deducted from final settlement). Don't sign blindly — but don't panic either; we'll cover handling this in the HR section.

> Tier-2/3 students ke liye NQT is the **one test that levels the playing field** with IIT/NIT students. Pure score matters, brand of college doesn't.

### 2.4 Adaptive vs non-adaptive — TCS NQT is non-adaptive

This is critical: **TCS NQT is NOT adaptive**. Sab questions deterministic hain. Galat answer ka next question harder nahi hota (unlike AMCAT). Iska matlab:

- Negative marking? **No.** TCS NQT mein negative marking nahi hai (2024 onwards confirmed).
- Skip question = 0 marks, attempt galat = 0 marks. So **always guess** — random guess pe 25% probability of getting it right.
- Backward navigation? **Yes** within section, **no** across sections. So agar Numerical mein Q5 stuck hai, Q25 ke baad wapas Q5 pe aa sakta hai. But ek baar Verbal section open kar liya, Numerical band ho jaata hai.

### 2.5 Score reporting — percentile to interview shortlist

TCS gives you a **score out of 100 per section + composite**. Interview shortlist works like this:

- Composite ≥60 → Ninja interview shortlist (basic cutoff).
- Composite ≥75 + Advanced cleared → Digital shortlist.
- Composite ≥85 + Advanced top 30% → Prime shortlist.

These percentiles are computed *across the entire NQT cohort* in your cycle (not just your college). Upper-quartile means roughly top 25% of ~250,000 attempted that cycle. Nationally competitive — har score pe lakhs ka pressure hai.

### 2.6 Section-skip myth busted

"Bhai Numerical chhod do, Reasoning + Coding mein top kar do" — **galat strategy**. TCS uses **section-wise cutoffs**, not just composite. Agar Numerical mein 30% laaya, total 80% bhi laaya, you'll still be filtered out at the Numerical cutoff (~40-45%). **Har section mein minimum 50% required hai. No section can be sacrificed.**

## 3. Numerical Ability — TCS-specific strategy

For fundamentals — formulas, shortcuts, justifications — see `aptitude-quant`. Yahan hum specifically TCS NQT pattern dekhenge.

### 3.1 Topic frequency (last 5 cycles, ~1500 questions analysed)

| Topic | Frequency | Avg Qs per test | Difficulty |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|------------|
| **Percentages, profit-loss, SI/CI** | 30% | 7-8 | Easy-Medium |
| **Time-Speed-Distance + Time-Work** | 25% | 6-7 | Medium |
| **Ratio, proportion, mixtures, ages** | 20% | 5 | Medium |
| **Number system, HCF/LCM, divisibility** | 15% | 3-4 | Easy |
| **Mensuration (2D/3D), geometry** | 10% | 2-3 | Medium-Hard |

Notice: **Probability + P&C is rare in TCS NQT Foundation**. Wahan rakhenge to Advanced section mein dikhenge. Foundation mein zyada focus on direct-formula numericals.

### 3.2 Five traps unique to TCS NQT

1. **Negative options trap.** TCS frequently includes "None of these" / "Cannot be determined" as Option D. Don't rush to pick it — it's right ~10% of the time, candidates pick it ~25% of the time when stuck. **If your computation gives a clean answer matching A/B/C, pick that.**
2. **Ambiguous unit conversion.** "A train at 72 kmph crosses pole in 5 sec. Length?" Answer in metres. Trap option: same number in km. **Always convert: 72 kmph = 20 m/s. Length = 20 × 5 = 100 m.** TCS will offer both 100 and 0.1 as options.
3. **Hidden rounding.** "Compound interest on ₹10,000 at 8% for 3 years." Exact answer = ₹2597.12. Options: 2596, 2597, 2598, 2600. **Don't round mid-calculation** — compute fully, then round.
4. **"At least one" probability inversion.** If a question says "at least one X happens," compute `1 − P(no X)`. TCS provides the direct computation as a wrong option.
5. **Plural unit deception.** "Three pipes A, B, C fill tank in 10, 12, 15 hours respectively. All open. Time?" Trap: students compute LCM (60) and divide by sum (15). Right answer: 1/(1/10 + 1/12 + 1/15) = 1/(6/60 + 5/60 + 4/60) = 60/15 = 4 hours.

### 3.3 The 60-second rule

25 Qs in 25 min = exactly 60 sec/Q. But realistically:

- **First 10 Qs**: <30 sec each (direct formula). Bank 5 min.
- **Next 10 Qs**: 60-90 sec (medium). Costs 2 extra min.
- **Last 5 Qs**: 90-150 sec (mensuration / SI-CI weird wording). Costs 3 extra min.

Net: 25 min budget, 25 min spent. **Skip aggressively** if a question is taking >2 min. Mark for review and move on.

### 3.4 TCS NQT sample question (real pattern)

> A shopkeeper marks his goods 40% above cost price and gives 15% discount. Profit %?

Slow path: assume CP = 100. MP = 140. SP = 140 × 0.85 = 119. Profit = 19/100 = 19%.

Fast path: net % = 40 + (-15) + (40 × -15)/100 = 40 - 15 - 6 = **19%**. **Time: 8 sec.**

This "successive percentage" formula `a + b + ab/100` saves 30 sec per question across ~5 questions per test = 2.5 min cushion.

For more on percentages, mensuration, work-rate — `aptitude-quant` sections 4 through 9.

**What's rare in TCS NQT verbal**: poetry, literary fiction, dense academic philosophy. Their RC stays accessible — that's a gift, but the **time pressure** is the real challenge.

### 4.4 TCS-specific error-spotting traps

1. **Subject-verb agreement with "either/neither/each"** — "Either of the boys **is/are** going?" → **is** (singular).
2. **"One of the X who"** — "He is one of the students **who has/have** topped." → **have** (refers to plural "students").
3. **Article omission** — "She is **best** student" → "She is **the** best student."
4. **"Different from" vs "different than"** — TCS prefers British: **"different from."**
5. **"Less" vs "fewer"** — uncountable vs countable. "Less water, fewer bottles."

## 5. Reasoning Ability — TCS-specific strategy

For fundamentals on coding-decoding, blood relations, syllogisms, puzzles — see `aptitude-logical`. Yahan TCS-specific lean.

### 5.1 The 100-second math

30 Qs in 50 min = 100 sec/Q. But **TCS Reasoning is the easiest section to score 90%+** if you're disciplined. Reasoning is pattern-based — once you've seen 5 versions of a question type, the 6th takes 30 sec.

### 5.2 TCS-specific topic distribution

| Topic | Frequency | Avg Qs |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| **Data Sufficiency** | 25% | 7-8 |
| **Statement-Conclusion / Statement-Assumption** | 20% | 6 |
| **Arrangement puzzles (linear, circular)** | 20% | 5-6 |
| **Coding-decoding** | 10% | 3 |
| **Blood relations** | 8% | 2-3 |
| **Direction sense** | 7% | 2 |
| **Syllogism** | 5% | 1-2 |
| **Series (number / letter)** | 5% | 1-2 |

Notice: **Data Sufficiency + Statement reasoning = 45%** of the section. TCS leans heavily here. AMCAT / eLitmus mein puzzles dominate; TCS mein DS + Statements dominate.

### 5.3 Data Sufficiency — TCS's favourite

Format: Question + 2 statements. Answer choices:
- (A) Statement 1 alone is sufficient
- (B) Statement 2 alone is sufficient
- (C) Both required together
- (D) Either alone sufficient
- (E) Neither sufficient

> **Question:** What is the value of x?
> Statement 1: x² = 16
> Statement 2: x is positive

Statement 1 alone: x = ±4 → not unique → insufficient.
Statement 2 alone: x is positive → not unique → insufficient.
Both: x = +4 → unique → sufficient. **Answer: C.**

> **Common trap:** Don't actually solve. Just decide *if* the statement is sufficient. Wasting 2 min on full computation = 2 questions ka time gone.

### 5.4 Statement-Conclusion vs Statement-Assumption

- **Statement-Conclusion**: "Given the statement, which conclusion *necessarily* follows?" Answer must be **logically deducible**.
- **Statement-Assumption**: "Which assumption *underlies* the statement?" Answer must be a **prerequisite for the statement to make sense**.

TCS loves the trap where the conclusion is "true in real world" but **not deducible from the given statement**. Always restrict yourself to the given text.

### 5.5 Arrangement puzzles — the 4-min budget

Linear / circular arrangement puzzles take ~4-5 min each. TCS gives 2 such puzzles per test, each with 4-5 questions. **Budget: 8-10 min for 8-10 questions** = great ROI.

Strategy: draw the diagram **fully** before reading questions. Don't half-solve and hope to finish. Once diagram is locked, all 5 questions answer in 30 sec each.

For full puzzle technique → `aptitude-logical` section 6.

### 5.6 Reasoning section TCS strategy summary

- **First 10 min**: Knock off 12-15 easy questions (coding, blood relations, directions) at 60-80 sec each.
- **Next 25 min**: Solve both arrangement puzzles fully. ~8-10 questions answered.
- **Last 15 min**: Tackle DS + Statement reasoning. These are interpretive — slow but mechanical.

**Target: 27/30 attempts, 22/30 correct = 73%**. Ye Digital cutoff easily clear karta hai.

## 6. Programming Logic Questions (PLQ) — the silent decider

10 MCQs in 15 min = 90 sec/Q. Sounds easy, but PLQ is **where engineering students lose marks they shouldn't**. Why? Confidence. Tu sochta hai "main coder hu, 90 sec mein output predict kar lunga." Aur woh 90 sec mein operator precedence ka edge case miss ho jaata hai.

### 6.1 Question type distribution (real TCS NQT pattern)

| Type | % of PLQ | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Output prediction (C/Java)** | 40% | What does this snippet print? |
| **Pseudocode tracing** | 25% | What is `result` after this loop? |
| **Time/Space complexity** | 15% | Big-O of this function? |
| **Conceptual (data structures)** | 10% | Which DS is best for X? |
| **Bug-spotting** | 10% | Which line has the error? |

TCS uses **C / Java / pseudocode** — not Python — in PLQ. So even agar tu Python-only coder hai, **C/Java syntax recognise karna padega** for output prediction.

### 6.2 Top 10 gotchas (memorise)

1. **Operator precedence**: `int x = 5 + 3 * 2;` → x = 11, not 16. `*` before `+`.
2. **Post vs pre-increment**: `int a=5; int b = a++;` → b = 5, a = 6 (post = use then increment).
3. **Integer overflow** in C: `int` is 32-bit signed. `2^31 - 1 = 2147483647` is max. Adding 1 → -2147483648 (wrap-around).
4. **Floating-point comparison**: `0.1 + 0.2 == 0.3` → **false** in C/Java (0.30000000000000004).
5. **String == in Java**: compares references, not content. Use `.equals()`.
6. **Array indexing**: `int arr[5]` has indices 0-4. `arr[5]` is undefined behaviour in C.
7. **Recursion base case**: missing base case → stack overflow.
8. **Switch fall-through**: missing `break` → execution continues to next case.
9. **Logical short-circuit**: `if (p != null && p.next != null)` — if `p` is null, second clause never evaluated.
10. **Bitwise vs logical**: `&` (bitwise) vs `&&` (logical). `5 & 3 = 1`, but `5 && 3 = true`.

### 6.3 Ten worked PLQ examples

#### Example 1: Output prediction (C)
```c
int main() {
  int a = 5, b = 10;
  int c = a++ + ++b;
  printf("%d %d %d", a, b, c);
}
```
Trace: `a++` returns 5, then a=6. `++b` makes b=11, returns 11. So c = 5 + 11 = 16. Print: `6 11 16`.

#### Example 2: Pseudocode tracing
```
n = 10
sum = 0
for i = 1 to n:
  if i % 2 == 0:
    sum = sum + i
print sum
```
Sum of even numbers 1-10 = 2+4+6+8+10 = **30**.

#### Example 3: Complexity
```python
for i in range(n):
  for j in range(i):
    print(i, j)
```
Inner loop runs 0+1+2+...+(n-1) = n(n-1)/2 times. **O(n²)**.

#### Example 4: Java string trap
```java
String a = "hello";
String b = "hello";
String c = new String("hello");
System.out.println(a == b);
System.out.println(a == c);
```
Output: `true` (literal pool), `false` (new object). TCS loves this one.

#### Example 5: Recursion base case
```c
int f(int n) {
  if (n == 0) return 1;
  return n * f(n-1);
}
f(5);
```
Output: 5! = **120**. Classic factorial.

#### Example 6: Bit manipulation
```c
int x = 12;     // 1100
int y = 10;     // 1010
printf("%d", x & y);
```
1100 & 1010 = 1000 = **8**.

#### Example 7: Pointer / reference
```c
int a = 5, b = 10;
int *p = &a;
*p = b;
printf("%d %d", a, b);
```
`*p = b` modifies a (since p points to a). Output: `10 10`.

#### Example 8: Bug spotting
```c
int sum(int n) {
  if (n = 0) return 0;       // Line A
  return n + sum(n - 1);
}
```
**Bug on Line A**: `n = 0` is assignment, not comparison. Should be `n == 0`. Without this, sum returns 0 always (assignment gives 0, treated as false, so n becomes 0 and never returns 0).

#### Example 9: Switch fall-through
```c
int x = 2;
switch(x) {
  case 1: printf("one ");
  case 2: printf("two ");
  case 3: printf("three ");
  default: printf("other ");
}
```
Starts at case 2, no breaks → prints `two three other`.

#### Example 10: Complexity nested
```python
i = 1
while i < n:
  i = i * 2
```
Loop runs log₂(n) times. **O(log n)**.

### 6.4 PLQ time strategy

- 60 sec per question is the budget; you have 90.
- Use the spare 30 sec for **double-checking** your output prediction.
- If a question has nested pointers / complex bit manipulation, mark and move. Come back at end.

**Target: 8/10 correct = 80%.**

## 7. The Hands-On Coding Round — where Ninja becomes Digital

**This is the most decisive section.** 2 problems in 45 min. Easy + Medium typically. Tu agar dono compile + pass kar leta hai, you're golden. Agar ek pass hua aur ek partial — Ninja interview mein bachega. Dono fail = pure NQT score gone, irrespective of MCQ scores.

### 7.1 Format details

- **Languages allowed**: C, C++, Java, Python (3.x).
- **IDE**: TCS-iON browser-based editor. No autocomplete, basic syntax highlighting.
- **Test cases**: 5-10 per problem. Visible (1-2) + hidden (rest).
- **Partial credit**: Yes — passing 3/10 test cases gives 30% on that problem.
- **Compilation errors**: Cost time, no penalty in marks. But compile-test is slow on iON; aim for first-shot compile.

### 7.2 Why Python

For TCS NQT hands-on, **Python is the fastest language** for most candidates:

- Fewer LOC than C/Java (no type declarations, no semicolons).
- Built-in functions: `sorted()`, `set()`, `Counter`, `reversed()` — all save 10-15 lines.
- String slicing: `s[::-1]` reverses a string in 1 line vs 5 in C.
- Dictionary / list comprehensions speed up frequency-count problems.

Trade-off: Python is slower at runtime, but TCS test cases mein input sizes are small (N ≤ 10⁵), so TLE issue rarely arises in Foundation hands-on. Advanced coding mein Python sometimes hits TLE — there C++ is safer (we'll address in section 8).

### 7.3 Reading input — TCS uses `input()`

Critical: TCS doesn't use command-line args, doesn't use file input. They use **stdin via `input()`**. Common patterns:

```python
# Single integer
n = int(input())

# Two integers space-separated
a, b = map(int, input().split())

# Array of N integers
n = int(input())
arr = list(map(int, input().split()))

# 2D matrix R rows, C cols
r, c = map(int, input().split())
grid = [list(map(int, input().split())) for _ in range(r)]

# String input
s = input().strip()  # always .strip() to drop trailing newline / spaces

# Multiple test cases (T at top)
t = int(input())
for _ in range(t):
  # solve each case
```

**Edge case to handle**: if input format is ambiguous, **assume one item per line** unless space-separated is explicit. TCS's typical format is **N on line 1, N space-separated values on line 2**.

### 7.5 Five worked problems with Python solutions

#### Problem 1: Second-largest element

> Given an array of N integers, find the second-largest element. If all elements are equal, print -1.

```python
n = int(input())
arr = list(map(int, input().split()))

unique = list(set(arr))
if len(unique) < 2:
    print(-1)
else:
    unique.sort(reverse=True)
    print(unique[1])
```

**Time**: O(N log N). **Space**: O(N).
**Common mistake**: just sorting and printing `arr[-2]` — fails when array is `[5, 5, 4]` (returns 5, expected 4). Always dedup first.

#### Problem 2: String palindrome (case-insensitive, ignore spaces)

> Determine if a string is a palindrome. Ignore case and spaces.

```python
s = input().strip().lower().replace(" ", "")
print("YES" if s == s[::-1] else "NO")
```

**Time**: O(N). **Space**: O(N).
**Edge cases**: empty string after stripping → palindrome (return YES). Single char → palindrome.

#### Problem 3: Count vowels and consonants

```python
s = input().strip().lower()
vowels = sum(1 for c in s if c in "aeiou")
consonants = sum(1 for c in s if c.isalpha() and c not in "aeiou")
print(vowels, consonants)
```

**Common mistake**: counting digits / spaces as consonants. Always check `.isalpha()`.

#### Problem 4: Prime check

```python
n = int(input())
if n < 2:
    print("NO")
else:
    is_prime = True
    for i in range(2, int(n**0.5) + 1):
        if n % i == 0:
            is_prime = False
            break
    print("YES" if is_prime else "NO")
```

**Time**: O(√N). **Edge cases**: n=1 (not prime), n=2 (prime), n=0 (not prime), negative (not prime).

#### Problem 5: Climbing stairs (DP basic)

> You can climb 1 or 2 steps at a time. How many ways to climb N stairs?

```python
n = int(input())
if n <= 2:
    print(n)
else:
    a, b = 1, 2
    for _ in range(3, n + 1):
        a, b = b, a + b
    print(b)
```

**Time**: O(N). **Space**: O(1) — using two variables instead of array.
**Recognise**: this is Fibonacci. Same problem, different wording.

### 7.6 Edge cases checklist (always test these)

Before submitting, **mentally run** through:
- Empty input (string `""`, array `[]`)
- Single-element input
- All-same-element input (`[5, 5, 5]`)
- Negative numbers
- Maximum input size (N = 10⁵)
- Boundary: N=0, N=1
- Whitespace / case sensitivity in strings

### 7.7 Time strategy for hands-on round

- **First 5 min**: Read both problems. Decide which is easier. Solve easier one first.
- **Next 12-15 min**: Code easier problem. Compile-test against visible cases.
- **Next 25-30 min**: Code harder problem. Don't try to be fancy — brute force passes most TCS Foundation tests.
- **Last 5 min**: Re-test both. Fix any edge case.

**Target: Both problems passing 100% test cases. Even 80%+80% on both is better than 100%+30%.**

### 7.8 TCS coding round red flags

- Importing libraries that aren't in standard Python (no NumPy, no pandas).
- Using `eval()` or `exec()` — flagged as suspicious.
- Hard-coded test outputs (TCS hidden cases will fail).
- Infinite loops on edge inputs (causes TLE / runtime error).

## 8. Advanced Coding Round — gateway to Digital / Prime

If you've cleared Foundation, Advanced unlocks. **1 problem in 90 min.** This is where Digital (7 LPA) and Prime (9+ LPA) candidates separate from Ninja (3.36 LPA).

### 8.1 What "passing Advanced Coding" means

- Pass ≥80% of test cases in 90 min → **Digital eligible**.
- Pass 100% test cases in <60 min → **Prime shortlist consideration** (combined with Foundation top scores + interview).
- Pass <50% → still Ninja, no Digital boost.

The salary delta is real: Ninja at 3.36 LPA × 1.5 yr bond = 5 lakh total. Digital at 7 LPA × 1.5 yr = 10.5 lakh. **Doubling your salary for 90 extra minutes of effort is the highest-ROI activity in Indian campus placement.**

### 8.2 Common topics in Advanced Coding

| Topic | Frequency | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Dynamic programming** | 30% | Longest common subsequence, coin change variations |
| **Graph traversal (BFS/DFS)** | 25% | Shortest path in grid, connected components |
| **String algorithms** | 20% | Pattern matching, longest palindromic substring |
| **Greedy / sorting-based** | 15% | Activity selection, job sequencing |
| **Math + number theory** | 10% | Modular exponentiation, Sieve of Eratosthenes |

### 8.3 Three worked Advanced problems

#### Advanced Problem 1: Longest Substring Without Repeating Characters

> Given a string S, find the length of the longest substring with all unique characters.

```python
s = input().strip()
seen = {}
left = 0
max_len = 0

for right, ch in enumerate(s):
    if ch in seen and seen[ch] >= left:
        left = seen[ch] + 1
    seen[ch] = right
    max_len = max(max_len, right - left + 1)

print(max_len)
```

**Approach**: sliding window with hashmap. O(N) time, O(min(N, alphabet)) space.
**Common mistake**: not checking `seen[ch] >= left` — old indices outside window shouldn't matter.

#### Advanced Problem 2: 0/1 Knapsack

> N items, each with weight w[i] and value v[i]. Knapsack capacity W. Maximum value?

```python
n, w = map(int, input().split())
weights = list(map(int, input().split()))
values = list(map(int, input().split()))

dp = [[0] * (w + 1) for _ in range(n + 1)]
for i in range(1, n + 1):
    for j in range(w + 1):
        dp[i][j] = dp[i-1][j]
        if weights[i-1] <= j:
            dp[i][j] = max(dp[i][j], dp[i-1][j - weights[i-1]] + values[i-1])

print(dp[n][w])
```

**Time**: O(N × W). **Space**: O(N × W) — can be optimised to O(W) with rolling array.
**Trap**: indexing — `weights[i-1]` not `weights[i]` because dp is 1-indexed.

#### Advanced Problem 3: Number of Islands (BFS on grid)

> 2D grid of '1' (land) and '0' (water). Count number of connected islands (4-directionally).

```python
from collections import deque

r, c = map(int, input().split())
grid = [list(input().strip()) for _ in range(r)]

def bfs(i, j):
    q = deque([(i, j)])
    grid[i][j] = '0'
    while q:
        x, y = q.popleft()
        for dx, dy in [(-1,0),(1,0),(0,-1),(0,1)]:
            nx, ny = x + dx, y + dy
            if 0 <= nx < r and 0 <= ny < c and grid[nx][ny] == '1':
                grid[nx][ny] = '0'
                q.append((nx, ny))

count = 0
for i in range(r):
    for j in range(c):
        if grid[i][j] == '1':
            bfs(i, j)
            count += 1
print(count)
```

**Time**: O(R × C). **Space**: O(R × C) for queue worst case.
**Approach choices**: BFS (iterative, safer for large grids), DFS (recursive — risk of stack overflow if R × C > 10⁵).

### 8.4 Advanced Coding strategy

- **First 15 min**: read problem twice. Identify topic. Write 2-3 example traces by hand.
- **Next 15 min**: code brute-force. Get *any* working solution.
- **Next 30 min**: optimise if brute-force TLEs. Add memoization, switch to DP, prune branches.
- **Next 15 min**: edge cases — empty input, N=1, all-same, max input.
- **Last 15 min**: review code line-by-line. Test against visible cases.

**Target: 100% test cases or partial 70%+ for Digital eligibility.**

### 8.5 Python TLE risk

For large N (>10⁵) with nested loops, Python may TLE where C++ wouldn't. Mitigations:
- Use `sys.stdin.readline()` instead of `input()` for fast I/O.
- Avoid `.append()` in tight loops — preallocate lists.
- Use `dict` lookup over `list.index()`.
- For very tight loops, switch to C++ for the Advanced round only.

```python
import sys
input = sys.stdin.readline  # 3-5× faster I/O
```

### 9.3 Ten mistakes that auto-fail TCS email evaluators

1. **No subject line.** Direct email-body submitted. Auto-fail.
2. **"Hii Sir" / "Hi Mam"** — always "Dear Sir/Ma'am." Hi is informal; spelling "Hii" is unprofessional.
3. **No paragraphs** — wall-of-text email. Use 1-line opening + 3-4 line body + 1-line closing.
4. **Spelling errors** — "recieve / occured / definately." Run mental spell-check.
5. **Mixing Hindi + English** — "Sir aaj mein leave lena chahta hu, please permission do." Auto-fail.
6. **No closing line / sign-off** — email ending mid-sentence.
7. **All caps** — "LEAVE APPLICATION FOR THREE DAYS." Reads as shouting.
8. **Excessive emoji / smileys** in formal emails — even one ":)" can fail.
9. **Vague subject** — "Hello" / "Important" / "Urgent". Subject must contain the action.
10. **Asking for response without context** — "Please reply." Reply to what? Always specify the request.

### 9.4 Tone matrix

| Audience | Salutation | Sign-off | Body tone |
|----------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Manager | Dear [Name] | Thanks and regards | Direct, factual |
| HR | Dear Sir/Ma'am | Thanks and regards | Formal, polite |
| Peer (same level) | Hi [Name] | Thanks | Friendly, brief |
| Client | Dear Mr/Ms [Surname] | Best regards | Polished, no contractions |
| Senior leadership | Dear [Title] [Surname] | Sincerely | Formal, structured |

## 10. The HR Round — 5 minutes, 5 LPA on the line

TCS HR round is **fast — 3 to 5 minutes per candidate** in mass-hiring drives. They're not testing whether you're brilliant; they're testing whether you'll **stick around for 1.5 years**, **communicate clearly**, and **fit the TCS culture**. For STAR method depth and behavioural framework — see `resume-behavioural`. Yahan TCS-specific patterns.

### 10.1 The TCS HR archetype

TCS HR interviewers in mass drives are usually:
- 25-35 years old, 2-5 years TCS experience.
- Have a Q&A checklist they tick through.
- Take ~30 candidates per day, so they're tired by your slot.
- Score primarily on: communication clarity, attitude, location/relocation comfort, salary realism.

**Their goal**: filter out candidates who'll quit in 6 months. Their fear: false positive who joins and resigns.

### 10.2 The TCS HR question pack (8 must-prep questions)

#### Q1: "Tell me about yourself."

90 seconds, structured: education → 1 project → 1 strength → 1 reason for TCS.

> **Model**: "Hi, I'm Rohit, final-year B.Tech in Computer Science from XYZ College. My CGPA is 8.2. I've built three projects, the most exciting being a college bus-tracking app I deployed for 1500 students using React and Firebase. My strength is breaking complex problems into smaller deliverables — that helped me lead our final-year team. I'm excited about TCS because of its scale, structured training, and the diversity of projects across BFSI, retail, and digital."

#### Q2: "Why TCS?"

Don't say "because TCS hires in bulk" or "because of the brand." Be specific.

> **Model**: "Three reasons. First, TCS's scale — 600,000+ employees, 150+ countries — means the engineering challenges are unique compared to a smaller firm. Second, the ILP training program has a strong reputation for converting freshers into production-ready engineers in 3 months. Third, TCS Digital and CodeVita pathways give me a clear progression — I see a long-term career here, not just a first job."

#### Q3: "Are you open to relocation?"

TCS will post you **anywhere** — Chennai, Bangalore, Pune, Mumbai, Hyderabad, Kolkata, Indore. Saying "no" = auto-reject for most candidates.

> **Model**: "Yes, absolutely. I understand TCS has offices across India and projects can require travel. I'm comfortable relocating to any base location TCS assigns, and have already discussed this with my family — they're supportive."

#### Q4: "Are you okay with the 1.5-year service bond?"

Don't argue. Don't ask "what if I want to leave?" — that signals attrition risk.

> **Model**: "Yes, I'm fine with the bond. TCS's training and project exposure in the first year is itself worth the commitment. I see 1.5 years as a learning runway, not a constraint."

(More on the bond strategically — section 10.4.)

#### Q5: "What is your biggest weakness?"

Don't say "I'm a perfectionist" — TCS HR has heard this 1000 times. Be honest about a *correctable* weakness with a *fix-in-progress*.

> **Model**: "I tend to over-prepare before starting work — I want every detail mapped out before writing the first line of code. I've been working on this by setting a 30-minute discovery cap, then iterating in code. It's helped me ship faster in my last two projects."

#### Q6: "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"

TCS wants to hear: still at TCS. Don't say "I want to start my own company" or "I'm doing this till I get into MS abroad."

> **Model**: "In 5 years, I see myself as a Senior Systems Engineer or Tech Lead at TCS, ideally specialising in cloud or AI. I'd like to mentor 2-3 freshers, contribute to a major client engagement, and ideally pursue advanced certifications like AWS Solutions Architect along the way."

#### Q7: "Do you have any offers / are you interviewing elsewhere?"

Be honest but tactful. "Yes" is fine — most candidates do — but emphasise interest in TCS.

> **Model**: "I've appeared for two other companies, but TCS is my top preference because of the reasons I mentioned earlier. I'd love to confirm here as soon as the offer comes through."

#### Q8: "Any questions for me?"

**ALWAYS have 2 prepared.** Saying "no" looks disinterested.

> **Models**:
> - "Could you share more about the kind of projects ILP-completed engineers typically get assigned to in their first year?"
> - "What does growth look like in the first 2-3 years — are there opportunities to switch between domains like BFSI and Digital?"

### 10.3 Red-flag answers to avoid

- "I just need a job, any job." Honest but disqualifying.
- "I'll join TCS but plan to switch in 2 years." Don't volunteer this.
- "TCS salary is low compared to product companies." True but irrelevant in HR.
- "I don't like Java." If asked tech preference, frame as "open to all stacks, recent project was in Java."
- "My family won't allow relocation outside Maharashtra/Tamil Nadu/etc." Don't impose family constraints in interview.

### 10.4 The 1.5-year bond — strategic handling

The bond is real. ₹50,000 - ₹1 lakh penalty if you leave before 1.5 years. **In interview, accept it warmly.** Privately:

- 1.5 years gives you ILP training (3 months) + ~1.2 years of project experience. Valuable on resume.
- After 1.5 years, switch to product companies if you want — 1.5-2 years TCS experience is not a negative on resume.
- If you absolutely need to leave (medical / family emergency), TCS often waives bond on documented hardship.
- Paying the bond is also viable — ₹1 lakh is recoverable in 1-2 months of higher salary at the new company.

**Don't sign blindly, don't panic either.** Read the bond document during onboarding, raise specific concerns then — not in HR.

### 10.5 HR communication checklist

- Smile (yes, even on Teams).
- Eye contact (look at camera, not the screen of yourself).
- Pace your speech — don't rush.
- Pause for 2 seconds after question; don't rapid-fire.
- "Thank you" at start and end.
- Wear a collared shirt; clean background.

For STAR method on behavioural questions ("Tell me about a time when...") → `resume-behavioural` section 7.

- **`aptitude-logical`** — full reasoning depth: puzzles, blood relations, DS, syllogisms, 25 trickiest patterns.
- **`aptitude-verbal`** — RC technique, 11 grammar modules, vocabulary expansion.
- **DSA practice on LeetCode / HackerRank** — TCS interviews ask 1-2 medium DSA problems. 100 LeetCode Easy + 30 Medium = solid prep.
- **`resume-behavioural`** — STAR framework, 20 behavioural answers, resume polish, LinkedIn optimisation, salary negotiation.
- **`dbms-complete`** — TCS technical interviews ask SQL queries + normalisation + indexing.
- **`os-complete`** — process scheduling, deadlock, paging — common technical interview topics.
- **`networks-complete`** — OSI / TCP-UDP / HTTP — TCS Smart Hiring asks basics.
- **`java-fundamentals`** or **`spring-boot-basics`** — if your project / interview track is Java-heavy.

### 13.4 The mindset

Bhai, ek last baat. TCS NQT pass karna **achievement** hai, but it's not the destination. It's the *runway*. Iska 3.36 LPA Ninja offer ya 7 LPA Digital — ye numbers tujhe define nahi karte. **Kya define karta hai**: woh discipline jo tune 60 din practice mein dikhayi, woh confidence jo mock-after-mock build hua, woh structured email writing jo tu ab corporate context mein consistently kar sakta hai.

TCS ek launchpad hai. Tu agar isme top kar raha hai, tu Indian engineering ki top 5% mein aa gaya — irrespective of college brand. Aur ek baar tu join hua, agle 1.5 saal mein bahar dekhna start kar sakta hai — Wipro Elite Lateral, Infosys DSE Lateral, ya seedha product company switch — terre paas TCS experience + 1.5 saal of project depth hoga, jo fresher ke compared mein **massive** advantage hai.

Lage raho. Test crack kar. Joining letter le. Phir agle subject pe focus shift kar.

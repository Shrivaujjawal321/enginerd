# Verbal Aptitude — The Section Most Tier-2/3 Students Sleep On (and Pay For)

Bhai, ek baat clear kar le shuruwat mein. Tu jab kisi senior ko poochta hai "yaar TCS NQT mein kya tough tha?", woh hamesha bolta hai "Coding aur Quant". Verbal ka naam koi nahi leta — kyunki banda assume kar leta hai "main English samajhta hoon, hindi medium hu but Friends dekhi hai". Aur yahi pe **filter chup-chap kaat deta hai** — verbal cutoff miss = NQT fail = resume shortlist nahi.

Hard truth: TCS NQT, Infosys SP, Wipro Elite, AMCAT, eLitmus — sab mein verbal cutoff exist karta hai. Aur cutoff usually **60-65%** hota hai. Reading Comprehension under time pressure + sentence-correction grammar gotchas — ye dono cheezein hain jo tujhe usse aage le jaati hain ya rok deti hain. "I read English okay" ka illusion exam hall mein 12-minute RC passage padhte time toot jaata hai.

Iss subject ka motto: tujhe Booker Prize winner banane ka iraada nahi hai. Iraada hai ki tu **25 questions in 25 minutes** comfortably handle kar sake — yaani **60 seconds per question average** — and consistently 75%+ accuracy maintain kar paaye. Verbal mein ek rule yaad rakh — **example sentences and grammar rules ALWAYS in precise English**, kyunki ye English ka test hai. Hinglish narration is for *explanation between examples* only.

Hum 11 modules cover karenge — RC se idioms tak — har topic mein **slow vs fast technique** dono dikhayenge with timings. End mein top 25 trickiest sentence-correction patterns ka rapid table hai aur ek 80-sec-per-question survival checklist. Chai pani saath rakh, dictionary side mein rakh (initially), aur lage raho.

## 1. Why verbal kills tier-2/3 placements — the silent reaper

### 1.1 The structure tu face karega

| Test | Verbal Questions | Time | Cutoff (rough) |
|------|------------------|------|----------------|
| **TCS NQT Verbal** | 24 | 25 min | 60-65% |
| **Infosys SP English** | 22-25 | 25 min | 65% |
| **AMCAT English** | 18 | 16 min | 550 score (~60%) |
| **eLitmus Verbal** | 20 | 35 min | 60 percentile |
| **Wipro Elite Verbal** | 22 | 18 min | 55-60% |

Notice eLitmus — fewer questions but **35 minutes**. Why? Their RC passages are **longer and denser**. Other tests reward speed; eLitmus rewards careful reading. Strategy adjust karni padti hai.

### 1.2 The 60-second math

24 questions / 25 min = **62 seconds per question** average. But ye uniform nahi hai:

- **Synonym / antonym / one-word substitution**: 15-20 seconds. Pure recall.
- **Sentence correction / cloze**: 30-45 seconds. Rule application.
- **Para jumbles**: 60-90 seconds. Logical chaining.
- **Reading Comprehension** (1 passage + 5 questions): 8-10 minutes total. Yeh sabse heavy hai.

> **Pro tip:** TCS NQT verbal kholte hi seedha RC pe mat ja. Pehle saare short questions (synonym, error spotting, fill-in-blank) nipta — 12-15 questions in 8-10 minutes. Phir bachi 15-17 minute mein RC tackle kar with full focus. Reverse order = panic mode.

### 1.3 Common traps tier-2/3 students mein dekhe gaye

1. **"Sab options sahi lag rahe hain" trap** — sentence correction mein 4 options padhte hi confuse ho jaate ho. Solution: option **read karne se pehle** error identify kar. Eliminate by rule, not by feel.
2. **RC overthink trap** — passage mein har line tujhe deeply samajh aani chahiye, ye galatfehmi hai. Skim pehle, dive only when question demands.
3. **Vocabulary ratta trap** — Wren & Martin se 1000 words ratta maarna useless. Top 100 high-frequency words + root-word logic = 80% coverage.
4. **Hinglish-mode trap** — example sentence write karte time "He don't know" type errors. Exam mein woh option dabaega galti se. Drill precise English at example level.
5. **Idiom-as-literal trap** — "spill the beans" matlab beans giraana nahi hai bhai. Idioms memorise karne padte hain, logical deduce nahi hote.

### 1.4 Realistic expectations

- **Cutoff (TCS NQT, Infosys SP)**: 60-65%, achievable with 30 days disciplined prep.
- **Top-tier (TCS Digital, Infosys DSE)**: 80%+ — requires reading habit + grammar mastery.
- **Daily prep target**: 1 RC passage + 20 sentence correction + 20 vocabulary words. 30 days = 600 SC + 600 vocab + 30 RCs. Pattern recognition kicks in around day 15.
- **Free resources**: IndiaBix Verbal, PrepInsta, BYJU's Exam Prep, *The Hindu* editorial daily.

## 2. Reading Comprehension — the 10-minute battleground

RC is 5-7 questions in most tests, often 25-30% of total verbal score. Ek bhi RC galat ho gaya = pure section ka grade neeche. Pattern padhna hai, passage nahi.

### 2.1 Three passage types tu face karega

| Type | Tone | Question style | Strategy |
|------|------|----------------|----------|
| **Factual / Informative** | Neutral, dense with data | Detail-based, fact-finding | Skim for keywords, locate fact |
| **Argumentative / Opinion** | Author has a stance | Inference, tone, weakening | Read first + last para carefully |
| **Narrative / Descriptive** | Story or scene-setting | Main idea, character motivation | Track sequence and shift |

Modern TCS NQT mostly throws **factual** (tech / business / IPL / Swiggy / startup case studies). Infosys leans **argumentative**. eLitmus loves dense **academic / philosophical**.

### 2.2 Question types — recognise the verb

1. **Main idea / central theme** — "What is the passage primarily about?" Answer almost always paraphrases the **first or last paragraph thesis**.
2. **Detail / fact-based** — "According to the passage, X is..." Answer is **literally in the text**. No interpretation needed.
3. **Inference** — "It can be inferred from the passage that..." Answer is **NOT** stated, but logically implied. Common trap: an option that is "true in real world" but not implied **here**.
4. **Vocabulary in context** — "The word 'X' in line 12 most nearly means..." Choose meaning that **fits the sentence**, not dictionary's primary meaning.
5. **Tone / attitude** — "The author's tone is best described as..." Look at adjectives and adverbs the author uses. Sarcastic? Optimistic? Critical? Neutral?
6. **Strengthen / weaken** — "Which of the following, if true, would most weaken the author's argument?" Treat like Critical Reasoning (covered in section 7).

### 2.3 Three-pass reading technique

This is the single most useful skill in verbal prep.

**Pass 1 — Skim (45-60 sec):** Read first sentence of every paragraph + last sentence of passage. Get the structure, not the content. Tu bas yeh samajh — passage kis baare mein hai aur author ka stance kya hai.

**Pass 2 — Question scan (30 sec):** Pehle saare 5 questions padh le, BEFORE re-reading the passage. Underline keywords in questions. Now you know what to hunt for.

**Pass 3 — Targeted dive (5-7 min):** Read paragraph-by-paragraph, hunting for question keywords. Mark line numbers. Answer as you go.

> **Pro tip:** Never read the entire passage word-for-word twice. That's 4 minutes wasted. Pass 1 is structural map, pass 3 is targeted excavation.

### 2.4 Time budget per RC passage

| Activity | Time |
|----------|------|
| Pass 1 skim | 45-60 sec |
| Pass 2 question scan | 30 sec |
| Pass 3 dive + answer | 6-7 min |
| Buffer / verify | 30 sec |
| **Total** | **8-10 min** |

If you're at 12 minutes on one passage, you've already lost. Move to the rest.

### 2.5 Worked passage — Swiggy & the dark-store gambit

Padh isko exactly as you would in the test. I'll show you exactly how I'd attack it.

> **Passage:** When Swiggy launched Instamart in 2020, the prevailing wisdom in Indian e-commerce was that 30-minute grocery delivery was a niche urban indulgence, sustainable only at premium price points. Three years later, the company operates over 600 dark stores across 30 cities, each stocking 4,000-6,000 SKUs and turning inventory roughly twice a week. The economics, however, remain stubbornly fragile. Industry estimates place Instamart's contribution margin between negative 4 and negative 7 percent — meaning every order, even before factoring in central overheads, loses money.
>
> The optimist's case rests on three pillars: rising order frequency per user (currently 2.8 orders per month, up from 1.4 in 2021), shrinking dark-store payback periods (now 14 months from 22), and the cross-sell halo into Swiggy's core food-delivery business. Critics counter that quick commerce is a structurally negative-margin business — that customers' willingness to pay a delivery premium has a ceiling lower than the operational cost floor. They point to the bankruptcies of Gorillas in Europe and Getir's retreat from the US as evidence.
>
> A subtler argument is emerging from former Instamart executives: that the path to profitability runs not through increased volume, but through private-label margin capture. Instamart's house brands — currently 8% of GMV — earn 3-4× the margin of branded SKUs. If private label crosses 25% of mix, the unit economics flip positive. The question is whether Indian consumers, conditioned to brand-loyalty in groceries, will accept generic equivalents at scale.

**Question 1 (Main idea):** The passage primarily discusses:

(A) The history of dark-store retail in India
(B) The unit economics challenges and possible paths to profitability of Instamart
(C) Why quick commerce has failed globally
(D) The growth of Swiggy's food-delivery business

> **Solving:** Pass 1 told you the passage is about Instamart's economics. (A) is partial — only context. (C) overstates — it's mentioned but not the focus. (D) is wrong — food delivery is mentioned only as cross-sell. **Answer: (B).**

**Question 2 (Detail):** According to the passage, Instamart's contribution margin is currently:

(A) Positive but small
(B) Between negative 4 and negative 7 percent
(C) Around break-even
(D) Not disclosed

> **Solving:** This is literally stated in paragraph 1. **Answer: (B).** Detail questions are free marks — never miss them.

**Question 3 (Inference):** It can be inferred from the passage that:

(A) Swiggy will become profitable within the next 14 months
(B) Indian consumers currently prefer branded groceries to private-label alternatives
(C) Gorillas and Getir failed because of poor management
(D) Quick commerce will never be profitable in any market

> **Solving:** (A) — 14 months is dark-store payback, not company profitability. Wrong. (B) — passage says "Indian consumers, conditioned to brand-loyalty in groceries" — this is the inference. (C) — passage says they failed; doesn't say *why*. (D) — too absolute. **Answer: (B).**

**Question 4 (Vocabulary in context):** The word "stubbornly" in paragraph 1 most nearly means:

(A) Aggressively
(B) Persistently
(C) Foolishly
(D) Suddenly

> **Solving:** "Economics remain stubbornly fragile" — author is saying the fragility *won't go away*. Persistently = continuing. **Answer: (B).** Don't pick (C); "stubbornly" can carry a foolish connotation in casual speech, but here it modifies a state, not a person.

**Question 5 (Tone):** The author's tone is best described as:

(A) Enthusiastically supportive
(B) Sharply critical
(C) Analytically balanced
(D) Mockingly dismissive

> **Solving:** Author presents both optimist and critic positions, then introduces a "subtler argument" — clearly weighing evidence. Not cheerleading, not attacking. **Answer: (C).**

> **Pro tip:** Tone questions ka 90% answer "balanced", "cautiously optimistic", or "analytical" hota hai in business / factual passages. Test setters rarely make the author wildly emotional.

### 2.6 RC anti-patterns — what to avoid

- **Pre-loading opinions** — don't bring outside knowledge. If passage says "Mars is hotter than Venus" (factually wrong), and a question asks "according to the passage", the answer follows the passage.
- **Picking the longest option** — test setters know you do this. Length is uncorrelated with correctness.
- **Absolute words trap** — options with "always", "never", "only", "must" are usually wrong unless the passage explicitly states extremes.
- **Out-of-scope traps** — option introduces a concept not in the passage. Eliminate.

## 3. Sentence Correction — grammar fundamentals you must drill

5-8 questions per test. Pure rule-based. Once you internalise the rules, this is your highest accuracy section. Skipping these because "I know English" is the cardinal sin.

### 3.1 Subject-verb agreement

Rule: Verb must agree with subject in number (singular/plural) and person.

> **Example (basic):** *He plays cricket.* (singular subject, singular verb)
> *They play cricket.* (plural subject, plural verb)

**Trap 1 — intervening phrases:** Don't be fooled by phrases between subject and verb.

> **Wrong:** The bouquet of flowers *are* on the table.
> **Right:** The bouquet of flowers *is* on the table.
> **Rule:** Subject is "bouquet" (singular), not "flowers". The phrase "of flowers" is a modifier — ignore it for agreement.

> **Wrong:** The list of items *have* been updated.
> **Right:** The list of items *has* been updated.

**Trap 2 — collective nouns:** Team, family, government, committee, jury, audience, group. In Indian/British English, treated as singular when acting as a unit, plural when acting as individuals.

> *The team is winning.* (acting as one unit)
> *The team are arguing among themselves.* (members acting individually)

For TCS NQT, default to **singular** for collective nouns unless context clearly shows individual action.

**Trap 3 — "either/or", "neither/nor":** Verb agrees with the subject **closer** to it.

> *Neither the manager nor the employees were available.* (employees → plural verb)
> *Neither the employees nor the manager was available.* (manager → singular verb)

**Trap 4 — "each", "every", "everyone", "anyone", "nobody":** Always singular.

> *Each of the boys has completed the assignment.* (NOT have)
> *Everyone in the room is silent.* (NOT are)

**Trap 5 — "a number of" vs "the number of":**

> *A number of students are absent.* (a number of = many → plural)
> *The number of students is decreasing.* (the number = a single quantity → singular)

### 3.2 Tense consistency

Rule: A sentence shouldn't shift tense without reason.

> **Wrong:** He went to the market and *buys* vegetables.
> **Right:** He went to the market and *bought* vegetables.

**Reported speech rule** — when reporting verb is past, the reported clause shifts back.

> Direct: He said, "I am tired."
> Reported: He said that he *was* tired.

> **Wrong:** She said that she *is* coming.
> **Right:** She said that she *was* coming.

**Universal truths exception:** If reported clause is a universal truth, no backshift.

> *The teacher said that the sun rises in the east.* (NOT "rose")

**"Since" / "for" with present perfect:**

> *I have lived here since 2015.*
> *I have lived here for eight years.*
> NOT *I am living here since 2015.* (common Indian-English error)

### 3.3 Pronoun reference and ambiguity

Rule: Every pronoun must have a clear, unambiguous antecedent.

**Trap — ambiguous reference:**

> **Ambiguous:** When Rahul met Karan, he was wearing a blue shirt.
> (Who was wearing the shirt — Rahul or Karan?)
> **Clear:** When Rahul met Karan, Rahul was wearing a blue shirt.

**Trap — pronoun-antecedent agreement:**

> **Wrong:** Every student must submit *their* assignment.
> **Right (formal):** Every student must submit *his or her* assignment.
> **Right (modern accepted):** Every student must submit *their* assignment. (singular "they" is now widely accepted, but conservative tests still mark it wrong — use his/her in NQT)

> **Wrong:** The committee published *their* report.
> **Right:** The committee published *its* report. (committee = singular collective, takes "its")

**Reflexive vs object pronoun:**

> **Wrong:** My brother and *myself* went to the market.
> **Right:** My brother and *I* went to the market.
> **Rule:** Use "myself" only when subject and object are same person — *I hurt myself*.

### 3.4 Modifier placement — dangling and misplaced

Rule: A modifier should sit next to the word it modifies. Otherwise it modifies the wrong noun, often hilariously.

**Misplaced modifier:**

> **Wrong:** I saw a dog walking down the street wearing a hat.
> (Reads as if the street is wearing a hat.)
> **Right:** Walking down the street, I saw a dog wearing a hat.

**Dangling modifier (no logical subject):**

> **Wrong:** Walking through the park, the flowers looked beautiful.
> (Flowers don't walk.)
> **Right:** Walking through the park, I thought the flowers looked beautiful.

> **Wrong:** Having finished the assignment, the TV was turned on.
> (TV didn't finish the assignment.)
> **Right:** Having finished the assignment, Rohit turned on the TV.

> **Pro tip:** Whenever sentence starts with an "-ing" or "-ed" phrase followed by a comma, the **next noun must be the doer** of that action. If it isn't, dangling modifier.

### 3.5 Parallelism

Rule: Items in a list, or compared/joined elements, must be in the **same grammatical form**.

> **Wrong:** She likes swimming, dancing, and to read.
> **Right:** She likes swimming, dancing, and reading.
> (All gerunds.)

> **Wrong:** The course is interesting, useful, and it provides good examples.
> **Right:** The course is interesting, useful, and well-illustrated.
> (All adjectives.)

**Parallelism with correlative conjunctions** (either/or, neither/nor, not only/but also, both/and):

> **Wrong:** He is *not only* intelligent *but also* he works hard.
> **Right:** He is *not only* intelligent *but also* hardworking.
> (Adjective + adjective.)

> **Wrong:** She *both* sings *and* she dances.
> **Right:** She *both* sings *and* dances.

### 3.6 Comparative and superlative errors

Rule: Comparative (more / -er) for two; superlative (most / -est) for three or more.

> **Wrong:** Of the two brothers, Rahul is the *tallest*.
> **Right:** Of the two brothers, Rahul is the *taller*.

> **Wrong:** This is the *most unique* design I have seen.
> **Right:** This is a *unique* design.
> **Rule:** "Unique", "perfect", "complete", "absolute" are absolutes — they don't take "more" or "most".

**Double comparatives are wrong:**

> **Wrong:** This is *more better* than that.
> **Right:** This is *better* than that.

**"Than" comparison structure:**

> **Wrong:** My salary is higher than *my brother*.
> (Compares salary to brother.)
> **Right:** My salary is higher than *my brother's*.
> Or: My salary is higher than *that of my brother*.

### 3.7 Idiom usage — the SAT-style preposition trap

This is where ratta is unavoidable. Specific verbs / adjectives demand specific prepositions in standard English. Memorise these top patterns:

| Word | Correct preposition | Example |
|------|---------------------|---------|
| **Different** | from (NOT than) | This is different *from* that. |
| **Compared** | to (similarity) / with (analysis) | Compared *to* the rest. |
| **Capable** | of | She is capable *of* solving it. |
| **Comprised** | of (NOT comprised by) | The team is comprised *of* five members. |
| **Consist** | of | The book consists *of* ten chapters. |
| **Concerned** | about (worried) / with (involved) | Concerned *about* his health. |
| **Familiar** | with | I am familiar *with* the topic. |
| **Independent** | of (NOT from) | India became independent *of* British rule. |
| **Insist** | on | She insisted *on* paying. |
| **Prefer** | to (NOT than) | I prefer tea *to* coffee. |
| **Superior** | to (NOT than) | This model is superior *to* that. |
| **Inferior** | to | His work is inferior *to* hers. |
| **Conform** | to / with | The proposal conforms *to* policy. |
| **Comply** | with | You must comply *with* the rules. |
| **Distinguish** | between (two) / from | Distinguish *between* fact and opinion. |
| **Object** | to | I object *to* this decision. |
| **Accuse** | of | He was accused *of* theft. |
| **Charge** | with (an offence) / for (money) | Charged *with* murder; charged *for* services. |
| **Married** | to (NOT with) | She is married *to* him. |
| **Result** | in (cause→effect) / from (effect→cause) | Stress resulted *in* illness; illness resulted *from* stress. |

> **Pro tip:** TCS NQT verbal mein 1-2 questions guaranteed hote hain on these. Print this table.

## 4. Para Jumbles and Para Completion

3-5 questions per test. Logic-puzzle wala feel hai — sentences ka sequence pakdo.

### 4.1 The opening-sentence detection

The first sentence almost always has these features:

- **No pronouns referring to earlier mentioned things** ("he", "this", "such", "the said")
- **Introduces a noun with article "a"** rather than "the" (a → introduction; the → already known)
- **States a general principle, fact, or scene-setting**

> **Example:** Which is most likely the opening sentence?
> (A) However, this approach has limitations.
> (B) The startup raised its Series B in early 2023.
> (C) A new approach to payment processing has emerged in fintech.
> (D) Such criticisms, while valid, miss the point.
>
> (A) starts with "However" — contrast with something prior. (B) uses "the startup" — already introduced. (D) uses "such criticisms" — refers back. (C) uses "a new approach" — introduces fresh. **Opening: (C).**

### 4.2 The transition-word ladder

Transition words tell you what comes before AND after. Internalise this map:

| Transition | What it implies |
|------------|-----------------|
| **However, But, Yet, Nevertheless** | Contrast — previous sentence had opposite stance |
| **Therefore, Thus, Hence, So** | Conclusion — follows from previous |
| **Moreover, Furthermore, In addition, Also** | Continuation — same direction as previous |
| **For example, For instance** | Illustration — previous had abstract claim |
| **First, Second, Finally** | Sequence marker |
| **On the other hand** | Two-sided argument shift |
| **In contrast, Conversely** | Direct opposite |
| **Indeed, In fact** | Reinforces previous |

### 4.3 Four-step solving framework

1. **Identify the opener** using rules above.
2. **Map pronouns and "the"-references** to their antecedents — this gives you adjacent pairs.
3. **Use transition words** to chain forwards.
4. **Verify by reading the final order** — does it flow? Yes → submit. No → rework chain.

### 4.4 Worked example — 5-sentence jumble

> Arrange the following five sentences into a coherent paragraph.
>
> (P) However, the experiment failed to replicate at larger scales, casting doubt on the conclusion.
> (Q) Subsequent independent trials have produced mixed results, with three of seven labs reporting positive findings.
> (R) A 2019 study at MIT claimed that small doses of caffeine improved short-term memory by 15 percent.
> (S) The original team has since proposed that genetic variation in caffeine metabolism explains the inconsistency.
> (T) When tested on a sample of 200 students, the effect was statistically significant.

**Solving:**

- Step 1 — opener: (R) introduces "A 2019 study", uses "a", general framing. **Opener = R.**
- Step 2 — chain pronouns: (T) starts with "When tested" — refers to the study. Likely R → T.
- Step 3 — transitions: (P) starts with "However" — contrast with positive finding. After T's positive result, P provides the contrary. So R → T → P.
- Step 4 — (Q) "Subsequent independent trials" — comes after the original failure. R → T → P → Q.
- Step 5 — (S) "The original team has since proposed" — reaction to mixed results. R → T → P → Q → S.

**Final order: R, T, P, Q, S.** Reads cleanly.

> **Pro tip:** Agar do sentence dono opener-eligible lag rahe ho, dekh kaunsa **abstract / general** hai. General → first. Specific → later.

### 4.5 Para completion — the "fill the last sentence" variant

Yahan tujhe paragraph ka **logical conclusion** detect karna hai. Rule: the last sentence usually...

- **Summarises** the main argument, OR
- **Extends to implication / consequence**, OR
- **Restates the thesis with a twist**

Eliminate options that introduce **new topics** or **contradict the established direction**.

## 5. Sentence Completion / Cloze

3-4 questions. Fill blanks with right word/phrase.

### 5.1 Context detection — read the full sentence first

Don't just look at the blank's immediate neighbour. Read full sentence, then re-read with each option to feel the fit.

### 5.2 Contrast vs continuation cues

| Cue word | Direction |
|----------|-----------|
| **but, however, although, despite, yet, while, whereas** | Contrast — blank holds opposite of nearby idea |
| **and, also, moreover, similarly, likewise, in addition** | Continuation — blank holds same direction |
| **because, since, as, due to, owing to** | Cause — blank holds reason |
| **so, therefore, hence, thus, consequently** | Effect — blank holds result |

> **Example:** "Although the proposal seemed _____ at first, careful analysis revealed several flaws."
> "Although" = contrast. Second clause is negative ("revealed flaws"). So first clause must be positive.
> Options: (A) flawed (B) sound (C) ambiguous (D) hostile.
> **Answer: (B) sound.** Sound = positive opposite to "flaws".

### 5.3 Valence / polarity trick

When stuck, just classify each option as **positive (+)**, **negative (−)**, or **neutral (0)**. Then check which polarity fits.

> **Example:** "His remarks were so _____ that the audience left in dismay."
> Audience left in dismay = negative outcome. Remarks must be negative.
> Options: (A) inspiring (B) tedious (C) vivid (D) succinct.
> Polarities: + , − , 0 , 0. Only (B) is negative. **Answer: (B).**

### 5.4 Two-blank questions

Both blanks must be consistent with the same logical direction.

> **Example:** "The senator's speech, though ____ in delivery, was ____ in substance."
> "Though" = contrast between two clauses. So one positive, one negative.
> (A) confident, weak — fits.
> (B) confident, strong — both positive, no contrast.
> **Answer: (A).**

### 5.5 Cloze passage technique

Cloze = paragraph with multiple blanks. Read the **entire paragraph first** without options. Then fill blanks one by one — earlier blanks constrain later ones.

> **Pro tip:** Don't fill blanks in order. Easy ones first; they narrow context for harder ones.

## 6. Synonyms and Antonyms — strategy, not memorisation

3-5 questions. Pure vocabulary. But ratta lagana fool's game hai — root-word logic + valence elimination is the smarter play.

### 6.1 Latin and Greek root shortcuts

Knowing 30 roots covers ~60% of academic English vocabulary. Top picks:

| Root | Meaning | Examples |
|------|---------|----------|
| **bene** | good | benefit, benevolent, benign |
| **mal** | bad | malice, malevolent, malign, malady |
| **mit / mis** | send | transmit, dismiss, missile, emissary |
| **vid / vis** | see | vision, evident, visible, visualise |
| **scrib / script** | write | manuscript, inscribe, prescribe |
| **port** | carry | transport, import, portable |
| **dict** | speak | dictate, contradict, predict, verdict |
| **cred** | believe | credible, incredulous, credentials |
| **path** | feeling | empathy, sympathy, pathetic, apathy |
| **chron** | time | chronology, synchronise, chronic |
| **phil** | love | philosophy, philanthropy, bibliophile |
| **anti** | against | antibiotic, antithesis, antidote |
| **pre** | before | preview, predict, predecessor |
| **post** | after | postpone, postscript, posterior |
| **sub** | under | submarine, subordinate, subterfuge |
| **super / sur** | above | supervise, surface, surplus |
| **ambi / amphi** | both | ambiguous, ambidextrous, amphibian |
| **a / an** | not / without | atypical, anonymous, apathy |
| **omni** | all | omnipresent, omniscient, omnipotent |
| **multi** | many | multilingual, multifaceted, multitude |
| **mono / uni** | one | monopoly, monotone, unique, unanimous |
| **bi / di** | two | bilateral, dichotomy, dilemma |

> **Application:** "Malevolent" — "mal" (bad) + "vol" (will) → ill-willed. Synonym = malicious, hostile. Antonym = benevolent.

### 6.2 Eliminate by valence

When you're not sure about an option's exact meaning, classify as +, −, 0.

> **Question:** Synonym of **abhor**?
> (A) admire (B) detest (C) ignore (D) consider
>
> "Abhor" — even if you've never seen it, "ab" prefix often = away, separation. Strong negative feeling.
> Polarities: + , − , 0, 0. Only (B) negative. **Answer: detest.**

### 6.3 Antonym elimination

For antonyms, valence flips. Find the polarity of the question word, then pick **opposite-polarity** options.

> **Question:** Antonym of **frugal**?
> (A) thrifty (B) extravagant (C) careful (D) economical
>
> Frugal = careful with money (positive in conservative reading, neutral). Antonym should be its opposite — wasteful with money.
> (A), (C), (D) are all synonyms (positive frugality). (B) is the lone outlier. **Answer: extravagant.**

> **Pro tip:** "Three options are synonyms; one is the answer" pattern is common in antonym questions. Spot the odd one.

### 6.4 Common confusion pairs

| Word A | Word B | Difference |
|--------|--------|------------|
| **Affect** (verb) | **Effect** (noun) | "Affect" = influence; "effect" = result |
| **Accept** | **Except** | Accept = receive; except = excluding |
| **Adverse** | **Averse** | Adverse = unfavourable; averse = opposed to |
| **Allusion** | **Illusion** | Allusion = reference; illusion = false impression |
| **Eminent** | **Imminent** | Eminent = famous; imminent = about to happen |
| **Stationary** | **Stationery** | Stationary = not moving; stationery = paper goods |
| **Principal** | **Principle** | Principal = head / chief; principle = rule |
| **Complement** | **Compliment** | Complement = complete / pair; compliment = praise |
| **Discreet** | **Discrete** | Discreet = careful; discrete = separate |
| **Elicit** | **Illicit** | Elicit = draw out; illicit = illegal |

## 7. Critical Reasoning — the underrated game-changer

2-4 questions. Tests logical reasoning **about** an argument, not vocabulary.

### 7.1 Question types

1. **Assumption** — "The argument assumes which of the following?" Find the unstated bridge between premise and conclusion.
2. **Strengthen** — "Which of the following, if true, most supports the argument?" Add new evidence that helps the conclusion.
3. **Weaken** — "Which of the following, if true, most undermines the argument?" Find an alternate explanation or counter-evidence.
4. **Inference** — "Which conclusion follows from the passage?" Stick close to stated facts; don't add assumptions.
5. **Paradox / Resolve** — "Which best explains the apparent contradiction?" Find a fact that reconciles two seemingly opposed observations.

### 7.2 Four-step approach

1. **Identify premise(s) and conclusion.** Conclusion often follows "therefore", "thus", "hence", or is the main claim.
2. **Identify assumption** — what must be true for premise to lead to conclusion?
3. **Match question type** — assumption / strengthen / weaken / etc.
4. **Eliminate by relevance** — out-of-scope options first, then evaluate the remaining 2-3.

### 7.3 Worked example 1 — Weaken

> **Argument:** Companies that adopt remote-work policies report 20 percent higher employee retention than companies that mandate office attendance. Therefore, remote work causes higher retention.
>
> **Question:** Which, if true, most weakens the argument?
> (A) Office-based companies offer free meals.
> (B) Employees who already prefer working from home self-select into companies offering remote work.
> (C) Remote workers report lower productivity.
> (D) Some companies have switched from remote to hybrid policies.

**Solving:** The argument claims **causation** from a **correlation**. Classic flaw. Weakening means showing the correlation is misleading.

- (A) — irrelevant to retention.
- (B) — selection bias. People predisposed to stay (those who want remote work) join remote-work companies. So retention is high *because* of self-selection, not the policy itself. Direct hit on causation.
- (C) — about productivity, not retention.
- (D) — irrelevant.

**Answer: (B).**

### 7.4 Worked example 2 — Assumption

> **Argument:** The new highway between cities A and B will reduce travel time by 40 minutes. Therefore, more residents of city A will commute daily to jobs in city B.
>
> **Question:** Which is an assumption made by the argument?
> (A) Travel time is the primary barrier currently preventing commuting.
> (B) The highway will be free to use.
> (C) City B has more jobs than city A.
> (D) The highway construction will be completed on time.

**Solving:** Apply negation test — negate each option; if argument falls apart, that's the assumption.

- Negate (A): "Travel time is NOT the primary barrier." Then even with 40-min savings, people might not commute (other barriers — costs, family, housing, etc.). Argument falls. (A) is the assumption.
- Negate (B): "Highway has tolls." Argument can survive — toll might be acceptable.
- Negate (C): "City A has more jobs than B." Argument doesn't logically depend on this.
- Negate (D): "Construction delayed." Doesn't affect the conditional argument.

**Answer: (A).**

> **Pro tip:** The negation test is your magic key for assumption questions. Negate each option; the one that **destroys the conclusion** is the assumption.

### 7.5 Common CR traps

- **Reversed direction** — option weakens when question asks strengthen, or vice versa. Always re-check question stem.
- **Out-of-scope** — option introduces a topic not in the argument. Eliminate.
- **Too strong** — "always", "never", "all", "none". Real-world arguments rarely need absolute support.
- **Restates premise** — sneaky in strengthen questions. Restating premise doesn't add support; new evidence does.

## 10. Top 25 trickiest sentence-correction patterns

The patterns that repeat across TCS NQT, Infosys SP, AMCAT.

| # | Wrong sentence | Correction | Rule |
|---|----------------|------------|------|
| 1 | The team are playing well. | The team is playing well. | Collective noun acting as unit → singular |
| 2 | One of the boys are missing. | One of the boys is missing. | Subject = "one", not "boys" |
| 3 | Neither of the answers are correct. | Neither of the answers is correct. | "Neither" → singular |
| 4 | He is more taller than me. | He is taller than I (am). | No double comparative; "than I" formal |
| 5 | She is the most unique person. | She is a unique person. | "Unique" cannot be qualified |
| 6 | I prefer tea than coffee. | I prefer tea to coffee. | Idiom: prefer X to Y |
| 7 | He is married with a doctor. | He is married to a doctor. | Idiom: married to |
| 8 | This is different than that. | This is different from that. | Idiom: different from |
| 9 | He is senior than me. | He is senior to me. | Idiom: senior/junior to |
| 10 | He laid on the bed. | He lay on the bed. | "Lay" past = lay; "lie" past = lay |
| 11 | He insisted to come. | He insisted on coming. | Idiom: insist on + gerund |
| 12 | The reason is because he was late. | The reason is that he was late. | Avoid redundancy with "because" |
| 13 | Each of the students have a book. | Each of the students has a book. | "Each" → singular |
| 14 | Walking through the park, the flowers looked beautiful. | Walking through the park, I noticed the flowers looked beautiful. | Dangling modifier |
| 15 | She not only sings but also she dances. | She not only sings but also dances. | Parallelism after "not only / but also" |
| 16 | Either he or his brothers is coming. | Either he or his brothers are coming. | Verb agrees with closer subject |
| 17 | I am working here since 2020. | I have been working here since 2020. | "Since" → present perfect (continuous) |
| 18 | The committee published their report. | The committee published its report. | Collective takes singular pronoun |
| 19 | He said he is tired. | He said he was tired. | Reported speech backshift |
| 20 | Hardly had he sat down when the phone rang. | Correct as is. | "Hardly...when" inversion is right |
| 21 | The number of students are increasing. | The number of students is increasing. | "The number of" → singular |
| 22 | Between you and I, this is a secret. | Between you and me, this is a secret. | Object pronoun after preposition |
| 23 | He is one of those who likes pizza. | He is one of those who like pizza. | Relative pronoun follows plural antecedent ("those") |
| 24 | She returned back home. | She returned home. | "Returned back" is redundant |
| 25 | If I was rich, I would travel. | If I were rich, I would travel. | Subjunctive mood: "were" for hypothetical |

> **Pro tip:** Cover the right column. Read only the wrong sentence. State the rule out loud. Then check. Drill till you can identify all 25 errors in under 4 minutes.

## 11. Pre-test checklist + 80-second survival guide

### 11.1 Two-week prep plan

| Week | Focus |
|------|-------|
| Week 1 days 1-3 | Sentence correction rules (sections 3 + 10) — 30 questions/day |
| Week 1 days 4-5 | RC daily — 1 passage with 5 questions, 10-min timer |
| Week 1 days 6-7 | Vocabulary 50 words + 30 idioms |
| Week 2 days 1-3 | Para jumbles + cloze + sentence completion |
| Week 2 day 4 | Critical Reasoning — 20 questions |
| Week 2 day 5 | Full-section mock test (25 questions, 25 min) |
| Week 2 day 6 | Review weakest topic, drill 50 questions |
| Week 2 day 7 | Final mock + rest |

### 11.2 Night-before checklist

- [ ] Top 25 sentence correction patterns reviewed once.
- [ ] Top 30 idioms + 100 vocab words skimmed.
- [ ] One *Hindu* editorial read fully — keeps your reading muscle warm.
- [ ] No new topics to learn — only revision now.
- [ ] 7 hours sleep. Brain needs glucose and rest, not Red Bull.

### 11.3 Inside-the-test playbook

1. **First 30 seconds**: skim entire question list. Spot the RC passage(s) — note their location and length.
2. **Solve short questions first** (synonyms, sentence correction, fill-in-blank) — aim for 12-15 in 8-10 minutes.
3. **RC second**: tackle with 8-10 minutes per passage. Three-pass technique (skim, scan, dive).
4. **Para jumbles + critical reasoning third**: 60-90 seconds each.
5. **Last 2 minutes**: review marked questions. Don't change answers based on doubt — change only when you see a clear error.

### 11.4 The 60-second decision tree

When you read a question, in **first 5 seconds** decide:

- **Synonym / antonym you know cold?** → 15 sec. Mark and move.
- **Sentence correction with obvious rule violation?** → 30 sec. Apply rule.
- **Cloze with clear cue word?** → 30 sec. Use valence.
- **Para jumble?** → 90 sec budget. Open with rules first.
- **RC question?** → 60-90 sec each. Hunt by keyword.
- **Total blank?** → flag, move, return at end. Educated guess at last.

> **Never spend over 2 minutes on a single non-RC question.** Time spent over-thinking a sentence-correction is time stolen from RC, where each question carries higher stakes.

### 11.5 Common silly mistakes — pre-flight check

- **Reading "weaken" but answering "strengthen"**: always re-read the question stem.
- **Answer based on common knowledge instead of passage**: in RC, the passage is the only truth.
- **Picking the longest option**: length doesn't equal correctness.
- **Forgetting to read the second clause**: in cloze, both halves of the sentence matter.
- **Hindi-English literal translation**: "I am having a car" — wrong. "I have a car" — right. Common in tier-2/3 student responses.

### 11.6 Daily warm-up gym (15 min)

- Read one *Hindu* editorial or *Indian Express* op-ed.
- Note 3 unfamiliar words; look them up; use each in a sentence.
- Do 5 sentence-correction questions cold.
- Do 1 RC passage from a previous mock (re-attempt, focus on speed).

These are your free wins. RC stamina compounds — daily reading habit alone takes you from 60% to 80% over a month.
